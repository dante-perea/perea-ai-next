## Foreword: The First Time Portable Benefits Has a Federal Vehicle and a Counter-Vehicle

For nearly a decade, the portable-benefits debate lived almost entirely at the state level. Utah's S.B. 233[^43] (signed by Gov. Cox on March 23, 2023, effective May 3, 2023) was the first state in the United States to remove the provision of benefits as a factor in worker-classification tests, opening the door for app-based companies to contribute to worker-controlled benefits accounts without triggering misclassification risk. Tennessee followed in April 2025, then Alabama in April 2025, then a wave of 2026 introductions across nine additional states. The federal conversation, by contrast, was confined to research papers, hearings, and the occasional pilot bill — never a coordinated legislative vehicle.

That changed in July 2025. Within nine days of each other, the U.S. Senate received both the Republican package — Senator Bill Cassidy's Unlocking Benefits for Independent Workers Act (S.2210) introduced July 8, 2025[^1], joined by Senator Tim Scott's Modern Worker Empowerment Act (S.2228) and Senator Cassidy's Independent Retirement Fairness Act (S.2217), both introduced July 9, 2025[^23][^35] — and the Democratic counter-vehicle, Senator Bernie Sanders's Pensions for All Act (S.2335), introduced July 17, 2025[^16][^17]. The Massachusetts Driver Portable Health Fund — a Stride/Uber/Lyft program born from the July 2024 Attorney General settlement — launched on April 1, 2025[^95], paying eligible Massachusetts rideshare drivers up to $1,074 per quarter in health-insurance stipends pooled across both platforms.

The 2026 portable-benefits debate is no longer about whether the federal government should engage. It is about which of three federal frames will win: Cassidy's voluntary safe harbor, Sanders's mandatory FERS-equivalent pension, or the EPI/NELP counter-coalition's argument that any federal portable-benefits framework legitimizes worker misclassification[^102][^104]. This paper synthesizes 114 primary and secondary sources to map where each frame stands as of mid-2026 — the bills, the cosponsors, the state replication map, the vendor-anchor empirical results, and the counter-coalition opposition.

## Executive Summary

The federal portable-benefits stack as of mid-2026 has six structural shifts that no other public source has assembled in one place.

**First, five federal bills are now pending in the 119th Congress.** Senator Bill Cassidy (R-LA), chair of the Senate Health, Education, Labor, and Pensions (HELP) Committee, introduced the Unlocking Benefits for Independent Workers Act (S.2210) on July 8, 2025[^1] — the legislative core of a four-bill package coordinated with Senators Tim Scott (R-SC) and Rand Paul (R-KY)[^3]. The package includes Scott's Modern Worker Empowerment Act (S.2228, July 9, 2025)[^23], Paul's Association Health Plans Act (S.1847, May 21, 2025)[^28], and Cassidy's Independent Retirement Fairness Act (S.2217, July 9, 2025)[^35]. Senator Bernie Sanders (I-VT), ranking member of HELP, introduced the Pensions for All Act (S.2335) on July 17, 2025[^17], referred to the Senate Finance Committee rather than HELP.

**Second, the House has a coordinated companion.** Representative Kevin Kiley (R-CA-3) introduced H.R.1319 Modern Worker Empowerment Act and H.R.1320 Modern Worker Security Act on February 13, 2025[^106]. The House Education and Workforce Committee marked up the bills on July 23, 2025[^108], voting 19-16 to report H.R.1320 to the full House. H.R.1320 was reported as H. Rept. 119-506 on February 20, 2026, and placed on Union Calendar No. 432[^106].

**Third, 18 states have introduced, enacted, or piloted portable-benefits legislation in three years.** Per the Mercatus Center's Liya Palagashvili catalog of March 18, 2026[^75], six states have enacted voluntary safe harbors (Alabama, Idaho, Tennessee, Utah, West Virginia, Wyoming); three operate active pilot programs (Pennsylvania, Maryland, Georgia); and nine introduced legislation in the 2026 session (Connecticut H.B. 5041, Florida H.B. 1431, Georgia H.B. 987, Hawaii S.B. 2088, Kansas H.B. 2602, Kentucky H.B. 732, Louisiana H.B. 301, Mississippi H.B. 1072, New Hampshire H.B. 1245, Rhode Island H.B. 7365).

**Fourth, the vendor-anchor empirical record is now substantial.** DoorDash's 12-month Pennsylvania pilot (April 2024 to April 2025) delivered $1.37 million[^90] to 4,400 eligible Dashers per the ndp|analytics report of July 26, 2025[^90]; 89% of participants without prior benefits felt more financially secure, and 97% would prefer the program become permanent. Lyft's 7% Utah pilot[^83] (January 2025 to March 2026) demonstrated the proof-of-concept for nationwide rollout. The Massachusetts Driver Portable Health Fund[^95][^98] (Stride/Uber/Lyft, April 2025 launch) paid up to $1,074 per quarter for drivers averaging at least 25 engaged hours per week pooled across both platforms — the first time U.S. rideshare drivers could combine cross-platform hours for benefit eligibility.

**Fifth, the counter-coalition is organized and active.** The Economic Policy Institute (EPI)[^102] and the National Employment Law Project (NELP)[^103][^104][^114] argue that the Cassidy/Scott/Paul package and the Kiley House bills "institutionalize a second-tier status for independent workers" and serve as "a veil for further misclassification." NELP's Laura Padin testified before the House Workforce Protections Subcommittee on May 16, 2025[^104], arguing that DoorDash's Pennsylvania pilot is "simply a savings account...rife with problems" because savings accounts are no substitute for insurance-based benefits.

**Sixth, two states have charted a hybrid path that combines IC classification with collective bargaining.** Massachusetts Question 3 (approved by voters November 2024)[^101] made Massachusetts the first U.S. state to allow rideshare drivers to unionize while remaining independent contractors. Illinois followed with the Transportation Network Driver Labor Relations Act (S.B. 2906 / H.B. 4743), filed January 20, 2026[^59][^66] — Uber agreed in June 2025 not to oppose the legislation[^63], a first major rideshare-platform concession on collective bargaining outside Massachusetts.

The structural prediction set: S.2210 likely passes the Senate by Q3 2027; Sanders's Pensions for All Act fails to advance; 5-7 additional states enact Utah-style voluntary safe harbors by end-2026; total Stride Save accounts cross 250,000 independent workers by end-2026; and one major rideshare platform announces nationwide voluntary contributions in 2027.

## Part I: The Stage — From Cassidy's June 2024 RFI to the July 2025 Bill Avalanche

The federal portable-benefits push of 2025-2026 did not emerge spontaneously. It traces to a single Senate request for information (RFI) issued nearly fourteen months before the first bill landed. Per the HELP Committee press release of June 5, 2024[^8], then-Ranking Member Cassidy requested feedback from "stakeholders on ways to modernize federal law to allow independent workers access to portable workplace benefits." Submission deadline was June 26, 2024, with responses directed to the IndependentWorkforce@help.senate.gov address. The RFI explicitly invited responses from "benefits providers, academic institutions, think tanks, labor unions, [and] worker advocates."

The synthesis of that RFI's responses became the HELP Committee whitepaper of April 16, 2025, titled *Portable Benefits: Paving the Way Toward a Better Deal for Independent Workers*[^6]. The whitepaper opens with an empirical anchor: "At least 27 million Americans engage in independent work, including truck drivers, construction workers, and direct sellers"; "the vast majority of independent workers prefer alternative work arrangements to traditional employment"; "80 percent of these workers would like access to portable workplace benefits"[^7]. It identifies the three competing federal classification frameworks — the common-law test (used for NLRA, ERISA, FICA), the economic-realities test (used by the Department of Labor under the Fair Labor Standards Act), and the ABC test (proposed in the PRO Act and applied by some state courts). The whitepaper notes that Department of Labor rulemaking on independent-contractor status "has been subject to several reversals, while the NLRB has overhauled its independent contractor test no less than three" times.

The whitepaper's load-bearing claim is that "while certainty on employment status is elusive under current law, it is certain that employers' provision of benefits to independent workers" can trigger reclassification[^6]. Cassidy's whitepaper proposes four reforms: a federal safe harbor for portable benefits, a single employment test, expanded access to Association Health Plans (AHPs) and Health Reimbursement Arrangements (HRAs), and expanded participation in retirement vehicles like Pooled Employer Plans (PEPs) and Single Employee Pension (SEP) IRAs[^7]. Per Cassidy's coverage in PSCA's analysis of April 23, 2025[^13], "only one in five independent workers participate in a defined contribution plan due to lack of access. This is the most significant barrier to savings for independent workers."

The federal regulatory backdrop matters. Per the Federal Register of January 10, 2024[^26], the U.S. Department of Labor published its final rule under the Biden administration rescinding the 2021 Trump-era Independent Contractor Rule and reinstating the "totality-of-the-circumstances" six-factor economic-realities test under the FLSA. That 2024 rule was one of the few Biden-era labor regulations to survive judicial review[^10]. In May 2025, however, the Trump-era Department of Labor announced it would "stop enforcing the Biden-era classification rule and revert to guidance issued in 2008"[^25], which created additional classification uncertainty and motivated the urgency of the Cassidy/Scott/Paul legislative package.

The bill avalanche concentrated in a 16-day window. May 21, 2025: Senator Rand Paul introduced the Association Health Plans Act (S.1847), with Senator Roger Wicker (R-MS) as the sole cosponsor[^28]. July 7, 2025: Cassidy, Scott, and Paul announced the coordinated legislative package via joint HELP Committee press release[^29]. July 8, 2025: Cassidy introduced the Unlocking Benefits for Independent Workers Act (S.2210) with six Republican cosponsors — Tuberville, Scott of South Carolina, Budd, Banks, Lankford, and Moody[^4]. July 9, 2025: Cassidy introduced the Independent Retirement Fairness Act (S.2217)[^35], and Scott introduced the Modern Worker Empowerment Act (S.2228)[^23]. July 14, 2025: Sanders's office circulated a draft of the Pensions for All Act[^15]. July 17, 2025: Sanders formally introduced S.2335[^16], the same day the HELP Committee held its hearing titled "Empowering Independent Workers: Strengthening Access to Portable Benefits"[^14].

The HELP Committee hearing of July 17, 2025[^14] is the only formal congressional record where Cassidy's frame sits alongside the Sanders counter-frame. Cassidy's prepared remarks emphasize that "states with Democrat governors like Pennsylvania, Maryland, and Wisconsin, and red states like Georgia, Utah, and Tennessee are all working on pilot programs or legislation that supports portable benefits" — a deliberate framing of the issue as bipartisan at the state level even where federal positioning has split. The hearing record bookends the legislative stage: by the end of July 2025, the federal conversation had moved from research papers and pilots to four Republican bills + one Democratic counter-bill + an in-flight House companion package + a coordinated committee hearing record. Every subsequent development — markups, vendor program launches, EPI/NELP counter-memos, the Massachusetts Driver Portable Health Fund — operates within the four-bill-plus-counter-bill structure that crystallized in those nine days.

## Part II: The HELP Package — Four Coordinated Bills

The Cassidy/Scott/Paul package is best understood as four coordinated bills targeting four distinct legal vectors — federal-law misclassification risk, FLSA/NLRA classification standards, ERISA-permitted health coverage, and ERISA-permitted retirement vehicles. Each bill is short (S.2210 is two pages, S.2228 is two pages, S.1847 is ten pages, S.2217 is twelve pages)[^2][^22][^27][^34], deliberately surgical, and designed to be passed individually if needed.

**S.2210 Unlocking Benefits for Independent Workers Act (Cassidy, July 8, 2025).** The legislative core of the package. Per the bill text published on GovInfo[^1], Section 2 reads in full: "A determination whether an individual is an employee of a person for the purposes of any Federal law shall be made without considering whether such person provides to the individual— (1) a benefit or protection that the individual may maintain without regard to whether the individual continues to perform work for such person; (2) a benefit or protection of a type that is commonly provided to a full-time employee; (3) a contribution, financial or otherwise, with respect to a benefit described in paragraph (1) or (2) that is— (A) made on behalf of the individual by the person in connection with work performed by the individual for the person; or (B) made by the individual; or (4) a benefit, protection, or contribution that is a combination of any benefit, protection, or contribution described in paragraph (1), (2), or (3)." S.2210 has seven Republican cosponsors per PolicyEngage tracking[^4]: Cassidy (sponsor), Banks, Budd, Lankford, Moody, Scott, Tuberville. Read twice and referred to HELP Committee 7/8/2025.

The bill is technically narrow but legally load-bearing. It does not create benefits, fund accounts, or mandate contributions. It only removes the provision of benefits as a factor in federal-law employee/independent-contractor determinations. Per PSCA's analysis[^9], "the mere provision of benefits cannot be used as a basis for categorizing that worker as an employee, which would give employers greater latitude to provide them to their contractors." Per HR Dive's coverage[^10], Cassidy's whitepaper acknowledged that "while certainty on employment status is elusive under current law, it is certain that employers' provision of benefits to independent workers" can trigger reclassification. S.2210 surgically removes that single trigger.

**S.2228 Modern Worker Empowerment Act (Scott, July 9, 2025).** Per the GovInfo bill text[^23], Section 2(a) amends FLSA Section 3(e)(1) (29 U.S.C. 203(e)(1)) by inserting "as determined under the usual common law rules" before the period; Section 2(b) amends FLSA Section 3(g) by inserting "an employee" after "permit." The effect is to replace the multi-factor economic-realities test (the Biden-era 2024 DOL rule preserved by litigation[^26]) with the common-law right-to-control test under FLSA. Per the Krevolin Horst legal analysis of May 28, 2025[^25], S.2228 "would focus on just two factors: significant control + worker discretion/judgment" — a "marked departure from the 'economic realities' test."

The Senate version is matched by the House version, H.R. 1319 Modern Worker Empowerment Act[^24], which goes further by amending NLRA Section 2(3) in addition to FLSA Section 3(e). H.R. 1319's two-factor independent-contractor test requires that (i) the entity does not exercise significant control over how the work is performed, AND (ii) the individual has the opportunities and risks inherent to entrepreneurship. The House version excludes from consideration: compliance with regulatory requirements, compliance with health and safety standards, insurance, and contracted-for performance standards. Senator Scott's bill has only one Republican cosponsor[^22], reflecting the narrower coalition for outright FLSA-test reform compared to the broader S.2210 safe-harbor approach.

**S.1847 Association Health Plans Act (Paul + Wicker, May 21, 2025).** Per the bill text[^28], S.1847 amends ERISA Section 3(5) (29 U.S.C. 1002(5)) to treat a group or association of employers as "an employer" regardless of whether the employers are "in the same industry, trade, or profession." Qualifying conditions per the bill: the association maintains a group health plan covering at least 51 employees aggregated across employer members; has been in existence for at least 2 years; is structured in good faith; and is not itself a health insurance issuer. Per Section 3(5)(C), a self-employed individual is treated as both an employer who may join the association and an employee who may participate in the plan. With at least 20 self-employed members, the association must charge a single risk-pooled premium rate.

S.1847 has bipartisan implications even without bipartisan cosponsorship. Per the joint HELP press release[^29], Senator Paul's framing emphasized that "the CBO previously estimated that 400,000 uninsured would gain coverage under AHPs and over 3 million people would switch coverage to AHPs." Per Certifi's analysis of June 30, 2025[^30], the historical AHP debate split insurers from small-business advocates: insurers worried about "good risks" exiting fully-insured plans for AHPs; small-business advocates argued AHPs gave small employers "leverage to negotiate collectively for lower health insurance and lower drug prices." Per the BenefitsPro coverage of May 28, 2025[^31], the Senate version was preceded by a House version (H.R. 2528 / Walberg) ordered reported by the House Education and Workforce Committee on June 25, 2025.

Senator Paul subsequently introduced a more expansive proposal — the Health Marketplace and Savings Accounts for All Act — on December 4, 2025[^32]. That bill raises the HSA maximum contribution to $24,500 for 2026, opens HSAs to all Americans regardless of income, expands HSA-qualifying expenses (insurance premiums, vitamins, gym memberships), and "allows any membership organization to sponsor an ERISA health plan across state lines, regardless of an employment relationship." The two Paul bills together represent the most aggressive expansion of association-based and individual-account health coverage in the 119th Congress.

**S.2217 Independent Retirement Fairness Act (Cassidy, July 9, 2025).** Per the GovInfo bill text[^35], the most operationally complex bill in the package. Section 6 establishes "Pilot Programs for the Gig Economy": Treasury and DOL "shall, in cooperation and after reviewing a survey of relevant academic literature and consulting with relevant companies, establish pilot programs to encourage independent workers to save for retirement." Two pilot programs are specified: (1) round-down compensation to nearest whole dollar with the excess automatically contributed to a Pooled Employer Plan, solo 401(k), or "suspension account"; (2) automatic deduction at independent worker's designated frequency (per pay period, monthly, quarterly, semi-annually, annually).

The bill defines a "suspension account" as "an account that is established and maintained on behalf of an independent worker that— (A) allows for the deposit of amounts by the independent worker, including the amounts described in paragraphs (1) and (2) of subsection (a); (B) allows for the independent worker to withdraw amounts deposited in the account and— (i) contribute such withdrawn amounts to a pooled employer plan in which the independent worker is enrolled; or (ii) contribute such withdrawn amounts into a simplified employee pension as provided under section 408(k)(10) of the Internal Revenue Code of 1986; and (C) provides that any amount remaining in the account at the end of each year, after any withdrawals under subparagraph (B), shall be returned to the independent worker in a lump sum." This is a novel federal vehicle designed for the irregular-income reality of independent work — funds accumulate during high-earning periods, are deployed to retirement during stable periods, and are returned at year-end if not deployed.

S.2217 also amends ERISA Section 3(43) and IRC Section 408(k) to allow independent workers to participate in Pooled Employer Plans (PEPs) "as if the worker were an employee of the employer in the plan, and allow trade associations to participate in the PEP as if the trade organization were an employer," per Ascensus's analysis of July 11, 2025[^37]. Per ASPPA's analysis of August 14, 2025[^38], the bill also "amends the groups of plans (GoPs) audit requirements" to permit consolidated audits "similar to the rules for PEPs" — a provision that resolves a pending ambiguity from SECURE 2.0 implementation. Per PlanSponsor's coverage of July 9, 2025[^5], "trade associations could act as surrogate employers, enabling gig workers to contribute to retirement plans as if they were company employees."

The four-bill package is internally consistent. S.2210 removes federal-law misclassification risk for benefit provision; S.2228 reforms the FLSA classification standard so that voluntary benefit provision is more clearly safe even before S.2210 takes effect; S.1847 expands the ERISA AHP vehicle so that benefits can flow through associations rather than individual employer-employee relationships; S.2217 creates retirement vehicles (PEPs, modified SEP IRAs, and the new suspension account) that match the irregular-income profile of independent work. Cassidy's framing in the joint package press release[^29] makes the case explicit: "Outdated labor laws should not prevent workers from receiving health care or saving for a secure retirement. Modernizing our federal labor laws ensures all independent workers can access workplace benefits without losing their flexibility to work how and when they want."

## Part III: Pensions for All — Sanders's FERS-Mandate Counter-Frame

Senator Bernie Sanders's Pensions for All Act (S.2335) inhabits a fundamentally different policy space from the Cassidy/Scott/Paul package. Where the HELP package creates voluntary safe harbors and additional retirement vehicles, S.2335 imposes a mandate. Per the bill text published on July 14, 2025[^15] and formally introduced July 17, 2025[^17], S.2335 was referred to the Senate Finance Committee — not the HELP Committee where Sanders serves as ranking member — a procedural choice that reflects the bill's tax-and-mandate structure rather than its labor-law overlap.

Section 3 of S.2335 reads: "Each employer shall— (A) make available to each employee employed by the employer a covered retirement program; or (B) notify the Secretary that the employees of the employer will participate in FERS." A parallel requirement applies to self-employed individuals: each self-employed individual shall either be enrolled in a covered retirement program or notify the Secretary of intent to participate in FERS[^17]. A "covered retirement program" is defined in Section 2 as "a retirement program other than FERS that the Secretary determines provides benefits that are comparable to the benefits available under FERS to an employee (as that term is defined under section 8401 of title 5, United States Code, on the day before the date of enactment of this Act)."

The structural pivot is the comparison anchor. Cassidy's S.2217 establishes voluntary participation in PEPs and SEP IRAs — both private-sector retirement vehicles already available under ERISA. Sanders's S.2335 sets the floor at the Federal Employees Retirement System, the defined-benefit plan that covers Members of Congress and federal civil servants. Per Sanders's press release[^16], the bill would require corporations to either "provide a traditional pension plan for their workers that is at least equivalent to the plan provided to new members of Congress under the Federal Employees Retirement System (FERS)" or "pay into the federal retirement system at a level that ensures all of their workers receive the same amount of retirement benefits as Members of Congress."

Section 4 of S.2335 amends Section 8401 of title 5, United States Code, to allow non-Federal employees and self-employed individuals to participate in the FERS Thrift Savings Plan. Section 5 establishes a credit for small employer and self-employed pension contributions, partially offsetting the mandate's cost burden. Section 6 imposes a penalty for failure to provide a covered retirement program — the enforcement mechanism that converts the mandate from an aspiration into a binding requirement[^15]. Section 7 prohibits employer reduction of compensation in response to the mandate, foreclosing the salary-offset workaround that has eroded other employer-mandate frameworks.

Sanders frames the bill as a supplement to his existing Social Security Expansion Act, which would apply Social Security payroll tax above $250,000 of income, raise benefits by $2,400 per year, and fully fund the program for 75 years. Per Sanders's statement at introduction[^16]: "We can no longer tolerate a rigged retirement system that allows the CEOs of large corporations to receive massive golden parachutes for themselves, while denying workers a pension after a lifetime of work. If we are serious about addressing the retirement crisis in America, corporations must be required to offer all of their workers a traditional pension plan that guarantees a monthly income in retirement. And if corporations refuse to offer a decent retirement plan, their workers must be allowed to receive the same type of pension that every member of Congress receives."

The endorsement coalition behind S.2335[^110] is union- and progressive-organization-led, distinct from the trade-association coalition behind S.2210/S.2217. Endorsements include the United Automobile, Aerospace and Agricultural Implement Workers of America (UAW); the Association of Flight Attendants-CWA (AFA); the Alliance for Retired Americans; Just Solutions; Equal Rights Advocates; Popular Democracy in Action; and NETWORK Lobby for Catholic Social Justice. UAW President Shawn Fain's endorsement statement[^111] frames the bill as a counter to "corporate greed" that "eroded" the pension foundation: "We've gone from being a country that promised security and dignity in old age to being a country that forces people to work until they're in the grave."

The empirical anchor in Sanders's framing is the 56-million-American population — "more than 56 million working-class Americans who currently have no retirement plan through their employer"[^16]. This population estimate sits between Cassidy's 27-million-independent-worker number[^7] and Stride's 72-million-American-independent-workers number[^56]. The discrepancy reflects definitional choices: Cassidy counts only independent contractors and freelancers; Sanders counts all working-class Americans (including some employees) who lack employer retirement coverage; Stride counts all Americans without any employer-based benefits regardless of work arrangement. Each of the three numbers is internally consistent with its source's framing — and each has implications for how the corresponding bill would scale.

S.2335's path is structurally closed. The bill was referred to Senate Finance, where the Republican majority has not scheduled a hearing or markup. Sanders does not have the procedural tools as ranking member of HELP to advance a Finance-referred bill. Per Common Dreams's coverage[^20] of the bill's introduction, the timing was deliberate — Sanders introduced S.2335 the same week the Trump administration was reportedly preparing an executive order to "give private equity vultures easier access to the 401(k) plans that have overtaken traditional pensions." S.2335 functions less as a vehicle expected to pass than as a counter-frame against both the Cassidy/Scott/Paul HELP package and the Trump executive-order approach to retirement-vehicle deregulation. Its endorsements, statement record, and bill text exist so that when the HELP package advances, the floor debate has a documented Democratic counter-vehicle to reference.

## Part IV: The House Companion — Kiley's H.R.1319 + H.R.1320

The House companion to the Senate package predates the Senate bills by nearly five months. Representative Kevin Kiley (R-CA-3), with cosponsor Mr. Messmer, introduced both H.R.1319 Modern Worker Empowerment Act and H.R.1320 Modern Worker Security Act on February 13, 2025[^106]. Kiley's framing is California-specific: per his press release at introduction[^107] and the RedState coverage of February 14, 2025[^115], "California's disastrous AB 5 law wreaked havoc on independent workers, stripping them of their ability to work on their own terms and forcing businesses to cut off contractor relationships."

H.R.1320 Section 2 reads: "Beginning on the date of enactment of this Act, for the purposes of any Federal law, a determination of whether an individual is an employee of a person shall be made without considering whether such person provides a benefit to the individual"[^106]. Section 2(b) defines "benefit" broadly to include "(1) a benefit, including a protection, that is provided to an individual for work performed for another person that the individual may maintain without regard to whether the individual continues to perform work for such person; (2) a benefit, including a protection, that is commonly provided to a full-time employee (such as workers' compensation, skills training, professional development, paid leave, disability coverage, health insurance coverage, retirement savings, and short-term savings); and (3) a contribution, financial or otherwise" by either the hiring person, the individual, or both. The substantive scope matches Senator Cassidy's S.2210, but with a slightly broader benefit-definition list that includes skills training, professional development, and short-term savings.

H.R.1319 amends FLSA Section 3(e) and NLRA Section 2(3). Per the bill text published on GovTrack[^24], the test it establishes is two-factor: an individual is an independent contractor if (i) the hiring person does not exercise significant control over the details of the way the work is performed (without regard to control over the final result) AND (ii) the individual has the opportunities and risks inherent with entrepreneurship (managerial skill, business acumen, professional judgment). The bill explicitly excludes from consideration: compliance with regulatory requirements, requirements to meet contractually agreed-upon performance standards (such as deadlines), and actions taken solely to comply with health and safety standards. Per the Pietragallo legal analysis of February 26, 2025[^109], the bill if enacted "would supersede the current final rule implemented under the Biden administration's Department of Labor (DOL) in 2024," which uses the six-factor totality-of-the-circumstances economic-realities test.

Per the Lebowitz analysis of March 3, 2025[^113], the most important constraint is scope: "These laws would apply only to classification under the FLSA and NLRA. The bills do not attempt to modify the IRS's Right to Control standard or any state law tests." That scope discipline matters. Independent contractors classified for FLSA purposes still face IRS classification using the traditional 20-factor right-to-control test, and state-law tests (most notably the ABC test in California, Massachusetts, New Jersey, and Illinois) remain unchanged. The Kiley bills would resolve federal-law ambiguity for benefit provision and FLSA wage-hour purposes, but they would not preempt the state-by-state classification battlefield documented in the parent paper on freelance finance.

The House Education and Workforce Committee marked up the bills on July 23, 2025, alongside H.R. 4366 Save Local Business Act (joint-employer reform), H.R. 4312 Student Compensation and Opportunity through Rights and Endorsements (SCORE) Act, and H.R. 4307 Enhancing Detection of Human Trafficking Act[^108]. Per the Committee Repository record, Roll Call Vote #6 on H.R. 1320 motion to report passed 19-16 — a roughly party-line vote. The bill was reported to the full House and placed on Union Calendar No. 432 as H. Rept. 119-506 on February 20, 2026[^106]. As of mid-2026, H.R.1320 is awaiting floor consideration; H.R.1319 is following a similar path.

The endorsement coalition behind the House bills is broad. Per Kiley's press release[^107]: the National Retail Federation (Edwin Egee, Vice President of Government Relations and Workforce Development) endorsed both bills as expanding "independent workers' access to benefits without placing into question their status as independent contractors." Stride CEO Noah Lang's endorsement statement[^107] frames the regulatory clarity case: "Stride supports the Modern Worker Security Act because it creates a safe harbor for companies, trade groups, and third parties to contribute to portable benefits — ensuring independent workers can access healthcare, paid time off, and retirement savings without compromising their flexibility. Our country's benefits system is broken, leaving out a large and growing portion of the American workforce, particularly in industries like trucking, nursing, gig work, freelancing, and seasonal employment." Stride is the most economically-aligned of the trade endorsements: every federal safe harbor that lands expands the addressable market for Stride's portable-benefits-administration platform.

The House bills face the same partisan structure as the Senate bills but with a reversed cadence. Where the Senate bills are bottlenecked by the 60-vote filibuster threshold (Republicans hold a working majority but not 60 seats), the House bills face only a simple-majority floor vote where Republicans currently hold a working majority. The path through the House is more direct than the path through the Senate; the path through the conference (assuming both chambers pass varying versions) becomes the structural choke point for 2026-2027.

## Part V: The 18-State Replication Map

If the federal package is the headline, the state-level replication is the substance. Per the Mercatus Center / Liya Palagashvili catalog of March 18, 2026[^75], 18 states have introduced, enacted, or piloted portable-benefits reforms in three years. The catalog is the single most cited source in the federal portable-benefits debate, and it organizes the state cohort into three operational tiers.

**Tier 1: Enacted voluntary safe harbors (six states).** Utah was first. Per the Utah S.B. 233 enrolled bill text[^43], the state enacted Utah Code 34-57-101 + 34-57-102 (Chapter 57 Portable Benefit Plan) effective May 3, 2023. Section 34-57-102 is the load-bearing provision: "(1) A governmental entity or private entity may offer a portable benefit plan. (2) Contributions to a portable benefit plan: (a) shall be voluntary; and (b) may not be used as a criterion for determining a person's employment classification. (3) If an Internet or application-based company contributes to a portable benefit plan for the benefit of an individual beneficiary: (a) the contribution is not evidence of employer liability; and (b) a court may not construe the contribution as an element of an employment relationship for purposes of: (i) Title 34A, Chapter 2, Workers' Compensation Act; or (ii) Title 35A, Chapter 4, Employment Security Act." Sponsor: Sen. John D. Johnson; House sponsor: Rep. Ryan D. Wilcox. Signed by Gov. Spencer Cox on March 23, 2023[^40].

Tennessee followed in April 2025. Per the Tennessee HB0494/SB1377 bill text[^46], the Voluntary Portable Benefit Plan Act enacts Tennessee Code 50-1-1101 et seq. Section 50-1-1103: "A public or private entity, including an internet or application-based company, may voluntarily contribute funds to a portable benefit plan as a form of compensation to an independent contractor. Contributions to a portable benefit plan must not be used as a criterion for determining a worker's employment classification under chapter 1, 2, 6, 7, or 8 of this title, or title 65, chapter 15." Effective April 3, 2025; Pub. Ch. 131. Per the Tennessee Senate Republican Caucus press of March 14, 2025[^47]: "More than 510,000 independent contractors in Tennessee — including gig workers, freelancers, consultants, and musicians" benefit from the law. Passed Senate 27-3, House 82-13 on March 20, 2025.

Alabama enacted SB 86 (Act 119) on April 10, 2025, signed by Gov. Kay Ivey, effective December 31, 2025[^53]. Per the Fisher Phillips legal analysis[^52] and the Stride blog coverage[^55], Alabama is "the first state to combine portability with full tax benefits." For tax years beginning after December 31, 2025, contributions to a Portable Benefit Account are 100%[^55] tax-deductible as a business expense for the hiring party AND excluded from the contractor's gross income. Per the Vensure HR analysis[^57], "businesses can deduct 100% of their contributions as business expenses. Contractors do not pay income tax on the benefits they receive." Idaho, West Virginia, and Wyoming round out the six-state enacted cohort per Mercatus[^75].

**Tier 2: Active pilot programs without statutory safe harbors (three states).** Per the Mercatus catalog and the Stride milestone post of July 15, 2025[^100], DoorDash launched its Pennsylvania portable-benefits savings pilot in April 2024 (the first such program); DoorDash launched a Georgia pilot in April 2025; and DoorDash launched a Maryland pilot in July 2025. Each operates without a statutory state-level safe harbor — but each is structured to fit within existing state IC classification rules so that the contributions do not, by themselves, trigger reclassification under the relevant state test. The Pennsylvania pilot's 12-month outcomes (covered in Part VI) provide the most-cited empirical foundation for the entire portable-benefits movement.

**Tier 3: 2026 introduced legislation (ten states).** Per Palagashvili's catalog and the related Mercatus state testimonies[^75][^78][^80][^82], the 2026 introduced cohort spans Connecticut H.B. 5041 (Governor's Bill, health-care-benefits version with Mercatus testimony submitted March 17, 2026[^80]); Florida H.B. 1431; Georgia H.B. 987; Hawaii S.B. 2088 (health-care-benefits framing); Kansas H.B. 2602 (with explicit tax advantage, Mercatus testimony submitted February 10, 2026[^82]); Kentucky H.B. 732 (Mercatus testimony March 18, 2026[^78]); Louisiana H.B. 301; Mississippi H.B. 1072; New Hampshire H.B. 1245; Rhode Island H.B. 7365.

The structural template across the 2026 cohort is consistent. Mercatus Recommendation Block 1[^76]: (1) decouple the presence of benefits from worker classification tests; (2) allow workers to register as independent + ensure regulator treatment; (3) Section 125 cafeteria plans + Universal Savings Accounts + state tax exemption. Six of the ten 2026-introduced bills incorporate at least the first reform; Kansas H.B. 2602 and the Alabama-style models incorporate all three. The Connecticut and Hawaii bills focus narrowly on health-care benefits, reflecting state-specific health-policy priorities rather than general benefits expansion.

The empirical Utah outcome is decisive. Per Palagashvili's Kentucky and Kansas testimonies[^78][^82], analysis of Utah post-SB 233 enactment shows "the number of W-2 employees continued to grow at a similar rate as before the enactment" of the portable benefits bill, and "the growth rate of self-employed workers remained unchanged." The portable benefits bill "had no impact on the growth of W-2 employees or self-employed workers in Utah" — the empirical refutation of the EPI/NELP misclassification-cover argument that voluntary portable-benefits frameworks accelerate worker reclassification. The Utah natural experiment is the central evidence the 2026-cohort sponsors cite when defending their bills against the EPI/NELP frame.

The federal-state interaction matters. States that have enacted voluntary portable-benefits laws operate in tension with federal IC classification rules: a state-authorized voluntary-benefits contribution is still legally exposed to federal misclassification risk unless and until S.2210 (or equivalent) is enacted. Mercatus and the National Retail Federation argue federal preemption (via S.2210's removal of benefit provision from federal employee determination) is the necessary structural complement to the 18-state replication. Without it, the state-level voluntary-benefits programs operate at the legal frontier where state-law safe harbors and federal-law misclassification risk coexist — and platforms operate cautiously as a result. The federal-state legal stack is the piece that explains why the federal bill avalanche of July 2025 happened: not because the state movement was insufficient, but because state success exposed the federal-law gap that only Congress can close.

## Part VI: Vendor Anchors — Stride, Lyft, DoorDash, Shipt, Uber

Stride Health is the technology infrastructure of the entire portable-benefits movement. Per Stride's PR Newswire release of August 19, 2025[^56], Stride is "the first portable benefits platform designed for the over 72 million American independent workers without employer-based benefits." The Stride platform — and specifically the Stride Save FDIC-insured no-fee interest-bearing savings account administered through the i3 Bank partnership[^96] — is the underlying contribution vehicle for every major platform-side portable-benefits program live in 2026. Per the Stride PR Newswire release of April 10, 2024 covering the Utah launch[^42], Stride had helped "more than 4 million workers save more than $5 billion on their taxes and monthly health insurance premiums" since 2014; per the Stride blog of December 9, 2024[^83], that figure rose to "more than 4 million workers save more than $7.2 billion" by year-end 2024; per the August 2025 Shipt announcement[^56], Stride "has helped 4.4 million people save over $8 billion on insurance premiums and taxes" and partners with "over 130 companies." Stride's growth trajectory tracks the underlying portable-benefits-program proliferation — as platforms launch programs, Stride captures the administrative layer.

**The Lyft Utah 7% pilot.** Per the Stride blog and Lyft corporate blog of December 9, 2024[^83][^85], Lyft and Stride launched a "first-of-its-kind program for Utah drivers" beginning January 1, 2025. Lyft contributed "a market leading 7%[^85] of an eligible driver's quarterly earnings (including bonus but excluding tips) into their Stride Save account." Eligibility required drivers to achieve "Elite" Lyft Rewards status by the end of the first month of each quarter and maintain it for the remainder of the quarter. Per the Lyft Help center[^84], the pilot was structured as a one-year opt-in program. Per the same source, "After March 31, 2026, our Portable Benefits pilot program with Stride will come to an end. Your Stride Save account will remain active." The last qualifying period was Q4 2025 (October-December); final contributions arrived between January and March 2026.

The Lyft Utah pilot is the most-cited proof-of-concept in the portable-benefits movement. Per Stride CEO Noah Lang's quote in the Fast Company exclusive of December 9, 2024[^88], "We've been fighting against the status quo for a long time. The dichotomy between you have to be an employee or an independent contractor. If you're an independent contractor, you can't have benefits. If you're an employee, you can't have the kind of flexibility as a contractor." Per Stride's data cited in Fast Company[^88], "36% of people in Utah have earned income, whether a lot or little, on an app-based platform" — establishing the addressable-market scale for Utah's voluntary safe harbor.

**The DoorDash Pennsylvania pilot.** Per the DoorDash press release of April 3, 2024[^89], DoorDash launched a "first-of-its-kind initiative to establish a portable benefits savings program for Dashers through a six-month pilot in Pennsylvania" with the support of Governor Josh Shapiro. The pilot ran from April 2024 through September 2024 originally, then was extended to a 12-month program ending April 2025. Eligibility: Pennsylvania Dashers earning at least $1,000 in pre-tip earnings during a qualifying quarter. Contribution rate: 4%[^89] of pre-tip earnings deposited into Stride Save accounts.

The 12-month results, published by ndp|analytics on July 26, 2025[^90], are the single most important empirical anchor for the entire portable-benefits debate. Headline data: "DoorDash deposited over $1.3 million[^90] into Stride Save accounts for 4,400 eligible participants in Pennsylvania's portable benefits pilot program. On average, participants received $296 throughout the pilot, but since the amounts depend on the dashing activity, the amounts received vary significantly. In particular, the top 10%[^90] earners received more than $580 in benefits and the top 1%[^90] earners received over $1,000 in benefits during the pilot." Total accumulated funds at end of May 2025 (one month after the pilot ended): $1,370,902 across all participant accounts ($1,301,771 in DoorDash deposits + $57,855 in self-contributed funds + $11,276 in interest).

The behavioral data are equally important. Per ndp|analytics[^90]: "Approximately 77% of survey participants reported feeling more financially secure due to the pilot program, and 91%[^90] expressed that they would feel even more secure if the pilot program were made permanent. After removing 30%[^90] of survey respondents who already have access to benefits through other sources, 66%[^90] of the remaining participants stated that the program provided them with benefits they would not have otherwise. Among this group, 89%[^90] reported feeling more financially secure due to the pilot program, and 97%[^90] would feel even more financially secure if the pilot program became permanent." Banking impact: "62%[^90] of participants who did not previously have [interest-bearing savings accounts]. Before opening an account with Stride Save, 14%[^90] of pilot participants did not have bank accounts, and another 48%[^90] only had checking accounts." The pilot delivered both portable-benefits contributions and bank-account access — a financial-inclusion outcome that the Cassidy package cites as the empirical foundation for federal preemption.

The classification preference data from the same survey are politically load-bearing. Per ndp|analytics[^90]: "Under the current situation (i.e., no portable benefits), 67% of respondents believe that app-based workers should be considered independent contractors, just as they are now. With the option of portable benefits, 82%[^90] of respondents would prefer the government to continue treating app-based workers as independent contractors. Among them, more than 54%[^90] also think that companies should be required to provide portable benefits savings." This is the data point Cassidy/Scott/Paul cite as the proof that voluntary portable benefits do not erode IC preference — the population that gains access to benefits remains 82%[^90] in favor of IC classification.

**The Shipt Alabama launch.** Per the Shipt corporate news of August 19, 2025[^51] and the Shipt PR Newswire release[^56], Shipt's home-state Alabama portable benefits program launched October 1, 2025 — timed to the December 31, 2025 effective date of Alabama's Portable Benefits Act SB 86[^53]. Contribution rate: 4% of pre-tip earnings (up to $300 per qualification window) per quarterly qualification period. Eligibility: $750 minimum pre-tip earnings during the qualification window. Per Stride's blog of April 11, 2025 covering the Alabama law[^55], Alabama's tax-deductible structure makes contributions "100% tax-deductible for the hiring business and excluded from the contractor's gross income."

**The Massachusetts Driver Portable Health Fund.** Per the Stride blog of April 8, 2025[^95], Stride was selected by both Uber and Lyft to administer health-insurance stipends to eligible Massachusetts rideshare drivers. The fund is a direct outcome of the July 2024 settlement agreement between the two companies and the Massachusetts Attorney General's office[^98]. The structural innovation: "for the first time that rideshare drivers will be able to pool hours across the Lyft and Uber platforms in order to qualify for specific benefits." Stride's role spans pooling driver hours, determining qualification, distributing stipends, and verifying enrollment in qualified health plans through the Mass Connector.

Stipend mechanics per Stride's MA stipend FAQ[^96] and the Mass.gov settlement FAQ[^98]: drivers averaging at least 15 Engaged Hours per week (combined Lyft + Uber) over a 3-month qualification period qualify for either a partial stipend ($537 per quarter for 15-24.99 hrs/week) or a full stipend ($1,074 per quarter for ≥25 hrs/week). The amounts are anchored to "the average cost of the lowest-cost Bronze tier plans available on the Massachusetts Health Connector for that period." Qualifying health plans must be subscriber-based, neither Medicare/Medicaid nor employer-sponsored. Quarterly periods: Q2 (April-June), Q3 (July-September), Q4 (October-December), Q1 (January-March beginning 2026).

**The vendor-anchor cohort summary.** Per Stride CEO Noah Lang's milestone post of July 15, 2025[^100]: DoorDash launched in Pennsylvania July 2024; Shipt launched in Utah July 2024; Lyft launched in Utah April 2025; DoorDash launched in Georgia April 2025; DoorDash launched in Maryland July 2025; Uber+Lyft launched portable health stipends in Massachusetts July 2025. Six programs across five states in twelve months, all administered through Stride Save. The structural insight: portable benefits has graduated from a single-program experiment to a multi-platform, multi-state, multi-cohort movement with a single dominant administrative layer (Stride). When S.2210 or equivalent passes, the Stride infrastructure is positioned to scale immediately to additional employer cohorts beyond rideshare and delivery.

## Part VII: The Counter-Coalition — EPI, NELP, and the "Misclassification Cover" Frame

The portable-benefits debate is not consensus. The Economic Policy Institute (EPI) and the National Employment Law Project (NELP) lead a counter-coalition that frames the entire Cassidy/Scott/Paul/Kiley legislative package as misclassification cover. Their core argument: voluntary portable-benefits programs codify a "second-tier" employment status by giving corporations a way to provide stingy benefits without reclassifying workers as employees with full FLSA, NLRA, and state-law protections.

EPI's framing came on July 17, 2025 — the day Sanders introduced the Pensions for All Act and the HELP Committee held its hearing. Per EPI's blog post by Billy Bonnist and Max Feist[^102]: "While misclassification of workers as independent contractors represents a significant problem, the legislative package fails to address this important issue in the gig economy. Instead, the bills would institutionalize a second-tier status for independent workers and entrench their exclusion from the full protections and benefits guaranteed to traditional employees." The Unlocking Benefits for Independent Workers Act would "help to cement independent contractor status for workers who, in many cases, should be entitled to employee status and the accompanying protections and benefits." EPI's frame: the rise of the gig economy is "not simply the natural result of a changing workforce but by the design of employers who seek to employ a labor force without the burden of providing the protections and benefits associated with employee-employer relationships."

EPI's policy alternative: "All types of workers genuinely need more access to benefits. We should support gold standard proposals to achieve this, not riskier plans that primarily benefit employers." Specifically: "If members of the Senate HELP Committee are truly committed to worker flourishing, they would crack down on misclassification, strengthen collective bargaining, and pass pro-union legislation." EPI does not endorse the Sanders Pensions for All Act explicitly — its framing places mandatory pension coverage in the same broad category as collective bargaining and pro-union legislation that would address worker security through employee status rather than through portable-benefits accounts.

NELP's framing is more operationally specific. Per NELP's March 6, 2025 statement (with July 7, 2025 update)[^103]: "Representative Kevin Kiley (R-CA) introduced two bills—the Modern Worker Empowerment Act and the Modern Worker Security Act—that do nothing to empower or enhance the security of working people. Under these bills, far fewer working people will have federal minimum wage, overtime and child labor protections, and the right to take collective action with their coworkers and join a union." NELP's specific objection to the Modern Worker Security Act: it "would exclude whether or not a business offers 'portable benefits' from being considered as part of the analysis of whether workers are employees or independent contractors under federal law. This is unnecessary because there is nothing prohibiting corporations from offering benefits to independent contractors. What this bill does do is legitimize a corporate-driven model where savings accounts with meager contributions from corporations are touted as 'portable benefits.'"

NELP's policy alternative is framed as "real" portable benefits. Per the same source[^103]: "Insurance-based portable benefits systems (unemployment insurance, Social Security Disability Insurance, and Medicare) and other insurance-based benefits (workers' compensation and employer-provided health insurance) pool risk, providing funds that help working people weather emergencies — such as loss of employment or serious injury or illness — that would otherwise be financially catastrophic. Insurance-based benefits are especially critical to workers in occupations with significant health and safety risks such as app-based delivery workers, a group of workers with the highest fatality rate in 2023." The NELP frame: portable benefits already exist (Social Security, UI, Medicare); the problem is that misclassified app-based workers are excluded from them; the solution is to reclassify, not to create a parallel savings-account system.

NELP's most operationally detailed critique came in Laura Padin's testimony before the House Workforce Protections Subcommittee on May 16, 2025[^104]. Padin, NELP's Director of Work Structures, testified: "DoorDash implemented what it calls a 'portable benefits savings pilot' program in Pennsylvania for its app-based delivery workers. The program is simply a savings account DoorDash sets up for eligible delivery workers, but is being promoted as an innovative way to provide workers with benefits — such as health insurance and paid time off — that they need." Padin's specific objections: "Savings accounts are no substitute for insurance-based benefits that employers fund. Insurance-based portable benefits systems, such as unemployment insurance, Social Security Disability Insurance" pool risk in ways savings accounts cannot.

Padin's House testimony explicitly rejected H.R. 1319 + H.R. 1320 + H.R. 4366 (Save Local Business Act). NELP's policy menu instead: "Passing laws ensuring all working people have the right to paid vacation, sick, and family and medical leave, and making accrued leave portable so that it follows workers from job to job; passing strong guardrails on AI in the workplace; adequately funding our existing portable benefits insurance programs, such as Social Security and unemployment insurance; promoting union-negotiated multi-employer benefits plans for workers that work 'gigs.'"

Padin's frame on the Cassidy bill from July 8, 2025[^114]: "This bill threatens to create a slippery slope that will enable the growth of Uber's business model — controlling workers through technological surveillance and algorithms while denying them employment rights and benefits. It would degrade work quality in many occupations that are already underpaid, difficult, and have high rates of misclassification and other labor violations, including homecare, ridehail, delivery, landscaping, construction, and janitorial work. It is no coincidence that Black, immigrant, and other workers of color — who face discrimination and occupational segregation that limits their job opportunities — are overrepresented in these jobs."

NELP also catalogs the state-level bills it considers misclassification cover. Per the NELP March 24, 2025 piece[^105], state bills that "define portable benefits account...narrowly to mean a particular type of savings program for independent contractors only" include Alabama HB 142, Arkansas SB 235, Florida SB 1130, Massachusetts HD 4210, Tennessee HB 0494, and West Virginia SB 714. NELP also identifies bills that "prohibit consideration of evidence relevant to employer/employee relationship," which include the Modern Worker Security Act + Alabama HB 142 + Arkansas SB 235 + Florida SB 1130 + Hawaii bill 1290 + Tennessee HB 0494 + Virginia SB 1276 + West Virginia HB 2852 + West Virginia SB 714. The NELP catalog is the most extensive list of state portable-benefits legislation framed from the opposition perspective.

The structural read for Part VII: the EPI/NELP counter-coalition is well-organized, well-resourced (NELP has a state-by-state Worker Policy Watch tracker), and operationally fluent. Its policy alternative — strengthen insurance-based benefits, expand collective bargaining, raise tax credits for IRAs and ACA exchange plans, mandate paid leave portability — is internally coherent and would address the same population the Cassidy package targets through entirely different legal mechanics. The 2026-2027 legislative debate is fundamentally about which of two visions wins: voluntary portable-benefits accounts that preserve IC classification, or mandatory expansion of insurance-based benefits + reclassification of misclassified workers as employees. The EPI/NELP frame ensures the second vision is documented and defended in the floor debate.

## Part VIII: The Hybrid Models — Illinois TNC LRA + Massachusetts Question 3

Two states have charted a third path between the Cassidy voluntary-safe-harbor frame and the Sanders/EPI/NELP mandatory-employee frame: collective bargaining for independent contractors. Massachusetts pioneered the model via Question 3 in November 2024[^101]; Illinois followed with the Transportation Network Driver Labor Relations Act (S.B. 2906 / H.B. 4743) filed January 20, 2026[^59][^66]. Both models preserve IC classification while creating a state-level legal framework for unionization — an architecture that exists nowhere in federal labor law because federal law (the National Labor Relations Act) restricts collective bargaining to W-2 employees.

**Massachusetts Question 3 (November 2024 ballot referendum).** Per the WBUR explainer of September 20, 2024[^101], Question 3 made Massachusetts "the first U.S. state to allow ride-hailing drivers to unionize." The mechanics: the state Employment Relations Board reviews driver-organization formation if it has 5%[^101] signed support from active drivers; once verified, the organization can obtain a list of other drivers from app companies; if 25%[^101] or more of eligible drivers give signatures to the formation, the union is recognized. Bargaining is statewide (sectoral). Union dues are voluntary; non-members may pay an "agency fee" depending on regulations. Per the same source, SEIU Local 32BJ was the main proponent of the ballot measure.

The Massachusetts framework operates alongside the Driver Portable Health Fund covered in Part VI. The fund itself is the product of a separate July 2024 settlement between the Massachusetts Attorney General's office (Andrea Campbell, who succeeded Maura Healey as AG) and Uber + Lyft. Per the Mass.gov settlement FAQ[^98], the settlement provided "back pay to drivers who used the Uber and Lyft Driver apps to provide rides to passengers in Massachusetts between July 14, 2020 and July 2, [2024]," plus new ongoing benefits: an automatic stipend for paid family and medical leave (effective January 30, 2025) and the health-insurance benefit (effective March 1, 2025, with hours starting to accrue April 1, 2025). The settlement preserved IC classification but added employee-style benefits — a structure that Massachusetts then extended via Question 3 to add union representation rights without changing classification.

**Illinois Transportation Network Driver Labor Relations Act.** The Illinois bill is more operationally sophisticated than the Massachusetts ballot framework. Per the Illinois Drivers Alliance legislative page[^66], S.B. 2906 / H.B. 4743 covers "100,000+ rideshare drivers throughout Illinois." Sponsors: Sen. Ram Villivalam (D-Chicago) + Rep. Yolonda Morris (D-Chicago). Coalition: Illinois Drivers Alliance, anchored by SEIU Local 1 + IAM Local 701 (Chicago Sun-Times[^59], CBS Chicago[^60]).

The bill mechanics involve a multi-stage authorization process. Per the Illinois Drivers Alliance bill description[^66]: 90 days post-enactment, all "covered TNCs" (those accounting for 95% of all TNC rides in the state in a given quarter, as determined by the Illinois Labor Relations Board) provide the Board with quarterly lists of drivers who completed five or more rides in the previous six months. The Board calculates the median ride count and designates any driver at or above the median as an "active driver." A driver organization must collect signed authorization cards from 10%[^66] of active drivers across all covered TNCs to access the full active-driver list. The organization is then certified as the exclusive bargaining representative; covered TNCs must bargain to a "sectoral agreement" that covers driver compensation, safety standards, deactivation appeals, paid leave, and dispute resolution. Ratification requires a simple majority vote among drivers who completed at least 100 trips in the prior calendar quarter. Voluntary union dues; no driver is required to join or pay dues.

The most consequential development is industry alignment. Per the Illinois Drivers Alliance press of December 9, 2025[^63]: "Uber has now agreed not to oppose bargaining rights for rideshare drivers in Illinois." This is the first major rideshare-platform concession on collective bargaining outside Massachusetts and removes the most-likely industry-side litigation threat to the bill's passage. Per the Sun-Times coverage[^59], the bill mechanics borrow heavily from the Massachusetts ballot model but adapt it to Illinois's labor-relations administrative architecture. Per the Block Club Chicago coverage[^61], Uber's June 2025 commitment "clearing the way for this week's proposal" was the result of "years of grassroots organizing in Chicago and across Illinois, including stalled attempts to raise driver pay at the city level."

The hybrid model's structural significance: it creates a third federal-policy outcome that neither the Cassidy package nor the Sanders Pensions for All Act addresses. Federal preemption of state collective-bargaining laws for ICs is unsettled — the National Labor Relations Act preempts state laws regulating collective bargaining among employees, but ICs are outside the NLRA's scope, leaving states free to regulate IC collective bargaining. If Illinois passes the TNC LRA in 2026 and additional states follow, the United States would have three coexisting frameworks for app-based workers: voluntary portable benefits (Cassidy / 18 states); mandatory employee classification with full benefits (Sanders / EPI/NELP); and IC classification with collective-bargaining rights (Massachusetts + Illinois + potential others). The 2026-2027 federal debate is structurally entangled with this state-level expansion.

## Part IX: 2026-2027 Predictions and the Counterfactual

The preceding parts establish the data points; this part articulates six time-bound predictions through end-2027 and two counterfactuals. Each prediction is anchored to a specific dataset disclosed in 2025-2026 federal filings, state legislation, or vendor-program disclosures.

**Prediction 1: S.2210 Unlocking Benefits for Independent Workers Act passes the Senate by Q3 2027 with 53-58 votes.** Per the seven-Republican-cosponsor list[^4] and Cassidy's HELP Committee chair position, the bill has the votes for a Republican-led majority. Per Sen. Manchin-style historical voting patterns and the Stride/National Retail Federation endorsements, 5-7 moderate Democrats are likely supporters in floor debate. The bill fails to clear the 60-vote filibuster threshold absent filibuster reform. Path: Senate HELP markup Q1 2026 (Cassidy as Chair); Senate floor consideration Q2-Q3 2027. House passage occurs first via the H.R.1320 Union Calendar No. 432 vehicle[^106] — likely Q2 2026.

**Prediction 2: Sanders's Pensions for All Act fails to advance.** Per the Senate Finance Committee referral[^17] and the Republican majority's procedural control, S.2335 receives no hearing or markup. Sanders does not have the procedural tools as ranking member of HELP to advance a Finance-referred bill. The bill's record exists for floor-debate counter-frame purposes and for inclusion in a future Democratic-majority retirement-policy package, not for 2026-2027 enactment.

**Prediction 3: 5-7 additional states enact Utah-style voluntary safe harbors by end-2026.** Per the Mercatus 2026-introduced cohort[^75], the highest-confidence candidates for enactment are Kansas H.B. 2602 (with explicit tax advantage), Kentucky H.B. 732, New Hampshire H.B. 1245, Florida H.B. 1431, and Rhode Island H.B. 7365. Lower-probability candidates (Connecticut H.B. 5041, Hawaii S.B. 2088) face structural opposition from state AFL-CIO affiliates. Per Palagashvili's testimonies in Kentucky and Kansas[^78][^82], the Utah empirical record (no impact on W-2 or self-employed growth) is the principal counter to the EPI/NELP misclassification-cover argument and is becoming a standard reference in state hearings.

**Prediction 4: Total Stride Save accounts cross 250,000 independent workers by end-2026.** Per Stride's milestone post[^100] and the cohort of in-flight programs (Lyft Utah ending March 2026, Shipt Alabama launching October 2025, DoorDash Georgia + Maryland + Pennsylvania ongoing, Massachusetts Driver Portable Health Fund expanding), the addressable population is at least 1.5M independent workers across these programs. Conversion rates from the Pennsylvania pilot (23% of eligible Dashers participated[^90]) imply 250-400K Stride Save account openings by end-2026 across all programs combined. The vendor-anchor cohort is the empirical test of whether portable benefits scale beyond pilot status.

**Prediction 5: One major rideshare platform announces nationwide voluntary 4% contributions in 2027.** Per the Lyft Utah 7% pilot proof-of-concept[^83] and the Massachusetts Driver Portable Health Fund operational infrastructure[^95], Lyft is the most-likely candidate to announce a nationwide voluntary-contribution program once federal preemption (S.2210) lands. The 4%[^95] contribution rate (DoorDash Pennsylvania, Shipt Alabama, DoorDash Pennsylvania bill proposal[^105]) is becoming the de facto industry standard; Uber's parallel infrastructure investment in Stride for Massachusetts[^95] suggests Uber follows shortly after. Federal preemption of misclassification risk is the necessary unlock for nationwide rollout.

**Prediction 6: Illinois TNC LRA passes in 2026; one additional state files a similar bill.** Per the Uber concession of June 2025[^63] and the Illinois Democratic-trifecta state government (House, Senate, Governor Pritzker), the bill faces a clear legislative path. Per the Massachusetts precedent and the SEIU coalition behind both states' efforts, the most-likely follow-on candidate is Washington State (where SEIU Local 775 has historically organized rideshare drivers) or California (where the post-Castellanos Prop 22 ruling preserves IC classification but allows additional state-level bargaining frameworks).

**Counterfactual A: Had Cassidy not become HELP Committee Chair after the 2024 election cycle, the federal portable-benefits legislative package would not exist.** Per the June 5, 2024 RFI[^8] (issued by then-Ranking Member Cassidy in anticipation of chair shift) and the April 16, 2025 whitepaper[^6] (issued in his first 90 days as chair), the entire federal stack was scheduled around the chair transition. A Sanders chairmanship of HELP would have produced a substantially different legislative package — most likely centered on Pensions for All Act-style mandates and ABC-test classification reform — with a corresponding counter-coalition led by Republican members and the Stride/NRF cohort.

**Counterfactual B: Had Massachusetts Question 3 failed in November 2024, the Illinois TNC LRA likely would not exist in its current form.** Per the Illinois Drivers Alliance press[^63] and the Block Club Chicago coverage[^61], the Illinois coalition explicitly references the Massachusetts model as the proof-of-concept that "unionize-while-IC" is a viable hybrid framework. A failed Question 3 ballot would have left the Illinois coalition without the industry-side concession (Uber's agreement not to oppose) and without the empirical example of a state-level legal architecture that doesn't require federal NLRA reform. The hybrid-model expansion path would have been delayed by years.

**Wild card: Trump administration DOL priorities.** The May 2025 reversion to 2008 IC guidance reduced federal classification stringency[^25], which paradoxically reduces the urgency of S.2210 for app-based platforms (since federal classification risk is currently lower). However, a future Democratic administration could reinstate the Biden-era 6-factor totality-of-the-circumstances test, restoring classification uncertainty and re-creating the urgency Cassidy's package addresses. The HELP package is best understood as a permanent legislative resolution to a regulatory-pendulum problem that has now reversed direction at least three times in seven years.

The 2026-2027 outlook resolves into one structural prediction set: the federal portable-benefits debate will produce a Senate floor vote on S.2210 by Q3 2027, fail to clear 60 votes absent filibuster reform, and remain unresolved at the federal level through the 2028 election. Meanwhile, state-level voluntary safe harbors will continue to expand (5-7 additional in 2026), vendor-anchor programs will scale to 250K+ accounts, and the Illinois TNC LRA hybrid model will create a third federal-policy outcome that neither the Cassidy package nor the Sanders counter-bill addresses. The portable-benefits legislative stack is at the inflection point where state experimentation has produced enough proof-of-concept to motivate federal action, but federal action remains structurally bottlenecked.

## Glossary

**ABC test** — A worker-classification test used by some state laws (most notably California, Massachusetts, New Jersey, Illinois) requiring that for a worker to be classified as an independent contractor, the business must demonstrate that (a) the worker is free from control and direction; (b) the work performed is outside the company's usual course of business; (c) the worker is customarily engaged in the work performed[^6][^67].

**AHP** — Association Health Plan. An ERISA Section 3(5) arrangement allowing groups or associations of employers to be treated as a single employer for purposes of offering health coverage[^28].

**Common-law test** — A worker-classification test that focuses on whether the hiring entity has the right to control the work performed. Used historically for NLRA, ERISA, and FICA purposes[^6][^23].

**Economic-realities test** — A worker-classification test used by the Department of Labor under the Fair Labor Standards Act, currently a six-factor totality-of-the-circumstances analysis (per the 2024 DOL final rule[^26]). The test asks whether the worker is in business for themselves or economically dependent on the hiring entity.

**ERISA Section 3(5)** — The provision of the Employee Retirement Income Security Act of 1974 (29 U.S.C. 1002(5)) defining "employer" for ERISA purposes; amended by S.1847 to expand association-based employer treatment[^28].

**FERS** — Federal Employees Retirement System under chapter 84 of title 5, United States Code. The defined-benefit plan covering members of Congress and federal civil servants; serves as the comparison anchor in S.2335 Pensions for All Act[^15].

**FLSA** — Fair Labor Standards Act of 1938. Establishes federal minimum wage, overtime, and child labor standards for employees. Section 3(e)(1) defines "employee," amended by S.2228 and H.R.1319 to harmonize with the common-law test[^23][^24].

**HELP Committee** — Senate Health, Education, Labor, and Pensions Committee. Chaired by Sen. Bill Cassidy (R-LA) in the 119th Congress; ranking member is Sen. Bernie Sanders (I-VT). Jurisdiction over S.2210, S.2228, S.2217 (referred to HELP); S.2335 was instead referred to Senate Finance[^17].

**IC** — Independent Contractor. A worker classified as self-employed for federal-tax and federal-labor-law purposes; not an employee. Subject to FLSA classification tests, NLRA classification tests, IRS Right-to-Control standard, and state-law classification tests (e.g., the ABC test).

**IRC Section 408(k)** — Internal Revenue Code provision establishing Simplified Employee Pension (SEP) IRAs; amended by S.2217 to allow independent workers to be treated as employees for SEP eligibility at the employer's election[^35].

**MWEA / MWSA** — Modern Worker Empowerment Act / Modern Worker Security Act. House bills (H.R.1319 / H.R.1320) introduced by Rep. Kevin Kiley (R-CA-3) in February 2025[^106]. Senate companion: S.2228 Modern Worker Empowerment Act (Scott).

**NLRA** — National Labor Relations Act of 1935. Establishes federal collective-bargaining rights for employees; ICs are outside its scope. Section 2(3) defines "employee," amended by H.R.1319 to use the common-law test[^24].

**PEP** — Pooled Employer Plan. A multiple-employer retirement plan typically reserved for traditional employees, established under the SECURE Act of 2019; amended by S.2217 to allow IC participation as if employees and trade associations to participate as if employers[^35][^37].

**Portable Benefit Account** — Worker-owned account established under state portable-benefits laws (Utah Code 34-57, Tennessee Code 50-1-1101 et seq., Alabama Act 119) to receive contributions from hiring parties without triggering employment classification[^43][^46][^53].

**Safe Harbor** — Legal mechanism that protects an action (here, the provision of voluntary benefits) from being used as evidence of employment status under federal or state law. The core mechanism of S.2210, H.R.1320, and the 6 enacted state laws.

**SEP IRA** — Simplified Employee Pension Individual Retirement Arrangement. A retirement vehicle established by an employer; current law restricts to employees only, amended by S.2217 to allow IC eligibility at employer election[^35].

**Suspension Account** — A novel federal retirement vehicle defined in S.2217 Section 6 that allows independent workers to deposit funds during high-earning periods, deploy them to PEPs or SEP IRAs at year-end, or receive any remainder as a lump-sum at year-end[^35].

**Stride Save** — FDIC-insured no-fee interest-bearing savings account product administered by Stride Health through i3 Bank partnership; the underlying contribution vehicle for DoorDash Pennsylvania + Lyft Utah + Shipt Alabama + Massachusetts Driver Portable Health Fund programs[^96].

**TNC** — Transportation Network Company. Statutory term used in Illinois, Massachusetts, and other states for app-based rideshare platforms (Uber, Lyft). The subject of the Illinois TNC Driver Labor Relations Act (S.B. 2906 / H.B. 4743)[^65][^66].

**UCL** — Unemployment Compensation Law. New Jersey's UCL is one of the laws governed by the NJ DOL ABC test final rule effective October 1, 2026[^67].

**Question 3** — Massachusetts ballot referendum approved November 2024 making Massachusetts the first U.S. state to allow rideshare drivers to unionize while remaining ICs[^101].
