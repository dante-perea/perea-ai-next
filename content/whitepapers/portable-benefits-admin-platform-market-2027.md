## Executive summary

The portable-benefits administrative platform market is no longer a one-vendor story. Stride Health still dominates the B2B2C lane — 4.6 million workers served, more than USD 8 billion saved, 130+ enterprise partners, and the only platform that has been chosen to administer state-mandated rideshare-driver health stipends in Massachusetts.[^1][^2][^3] But three other archetypes have emerged with material funding, regulatory standing, and live customer cohorts. Catch occupies the consumer-direct brokerage lane.[^4][^5] Solo Health Vault Captive (administered by Healthy Business Group through a North Carolina-domiciled captive) occupies the captive-insurance pool lane.[^6][^7][^8] Opolis occupies the employment-cooperative employer-of-record (EOR) lane.[^9][^10][^11]

The TAM is large and growing fast. The global gig-worker benefits platforms market reached USD 2.14 billion in 2024 and is projected to reach USD 7.86 billion by 2033 at a 15.3% CAGR.[^12][^17] The broader portable-benefits insurance market — a superset that includes group-portable products — was USD 64.7 billion in 2024 with a projected USD 162.1 billion by 2033 at a 10.8% CAGR.[^13][^17] North America accounts for ~USD 27.8 billion of the 2024 figure (43%).[^13][^17] The addressable population in the United States alone is over 60 million Americans (the figure Catch has used since 2021)[^14] or 72 million by Stride's 2025 count[^1] — between 18% and 22% of the US population, depending on the inclusion criteria for side-gig and contract work.

The state-level legal infrastructure that decides who can ship contributions into these platforms exists today in only four states (Utah 2023, Tennessee April 2025, Alabama April 2025, Wisconsin pending governor signature as of mid-2025).[^15][^16][^17][^18] The federal Modern Worker Security Act (introduced February 2025 in the House) and the Unlocking Benefits for Independent Workers Act (introduced July 2025 in the Senate) would create a national safe harbor.[^19][^17] Until either passes, the four-archetype vendor cohort competes inside a 50-state regulatory patchwork that strongly favors the platforms with the most diversified product surface and the broadest state-by-state operational footprint.

This paper is an authority survey. It catalogs the four archetypes, names the surviving vendors, prices the market, traces the policy timeline, and identifies the strategic dimensions that separate winners from losers in the 2026–2027 cohort.[^17][^18]

## The market

The global gig-worker benefits platforms market was USD 2.14 billion in 2024 and is forecast to reach USD 7.86 billion by 2033 at a 15.3% CAGR.[^12][^17] North America was the largest regional segment at approximately USD 950 million in 2024.[^12][^17] The broader portable-benefits insurance market (which includes B2B group-portable products in addition to the gig-worker subsegment) was USD 64.7 billion globally in 2024, projected to USD 162.1 billion by 2033 at a 10.8% CAGR; North America alone was USD 27.8 billion in 2024.[^13][^17]

The addressable worker population is the upper-bound demand signal. Stride cites 72 million Americans earning income through gig, freelance, or contract work in 2024.[^1] Catch cites 60 million Americans without access to employer benefits.[^14] The Senate HELP Committee's April 2025 white paper put the figure at "as many as one in three American workers" engaged in independent work, with 80% of independent workers wanting access to portable benefits.[^17] The independent-worker share of the US workforce has grown materially since 2017 — the last Bureau of Labor Statistics Contingent Worker Supplement — and is now estimated at "nearly 40% of the U.S. workforce" by Catch's 2021 Series A press materials.[^4][^14]

ICHRA adoption is the parallel structural shift. Individual Coverage Health Reimbursement Arrangements (introduced January 2020 under final HHS/IRS rule) had a slow pandemic-era start but reached approximately 1 million enrolled employees by 2025, with 60% year-over-year employer growth and 84% growth in the large-employer segment.[^20][^21] ICHRA is a portable benefits primitive: the employer contributes; the employee owns the underlying individual policy; coverage moves with the worker. Adoption among employers with 50+ workers grew 34% from 2024 to 2025.[^22]

## The four archetypes

The vendor cohort splits along a 2x2 of who administers and who underwrites. Stride and Catch administer benefits but do not underwrite (they sit on top of ACA-marketplace plans plus retirement and savings products from third-party carriers). Solo Health Vault Captive both administers and underwrites (a North Carolina-domiciled captive insurance company with reinsurance from Odyssey Reinsurance).[^7][^8] Opolis is the outlier — it neither administers benefits in the brokerage sense nor underwrites them; it converts solopreneurs into W-2 employees of a Colorado-registered Limited Cooperative Association so that the underlying group-rated benefits contracts apply.[^11][^9]

### Archetype 1: Consumer-direct brokerage (Catch)

Catch (Catch Financial, Inc., New York) raised USD 20.1 million across 5 rounds — including a USD 12 million Series A in July 2021 led by Crosslink Capital with participation from Khosla Ventures, Kindred Ventures, Nyca Partners, and Urban Innovation Fund — to provide health insurance, retirement plans, and tax withholding directly to freelancers without employer benefits.[^4][^5][^14] Catch's distribution strategy is enterprise partnerships: Freelancers Union, Upwork, and Lili Banking are documented partners, with the company describing itself as "the first and only portable benefits platform for independent workers."[^14] Catch is registered on the federal marketplace (Healthcare.gov) — one of only eight companies with this approval, and one of three that sells directly to consumers.[^14] Catch is operationally distinct from Catch.com.au, the Australian online retailer that Wesfarmers wound down in early 2025 with a USD 230 million write-off.[^23][^24] The two share a name only.

Catch's TAM (60 million Americans without employer benefits, per its own materials)[^14] is the addressable upper bound. Its actual penetration is unreported in public sources. The strategic question for Catch is whether the consumer-direct brokerage model can hit unit economics without a heavyweight enterprise distribution flywheel — Stride's central advantage.

### Archetype 2: B2B2C platform middleware (Stride)

Stride Health is the category leader and category definer. The company has saved 4.6 million independent workers more than USD 8 billion on insurance premiums and taxes, and works with 130+ enterprise partners.[^1][^2] Backing comes from Venrock, New Enterprise Associates, F-Prime Capital, Mastercard, Allstate, and King River, totaling USD 96 million in capital.[^25] Stride's distribution is enterprise-first: DoorDash, Uber, Lyft, Instacart, Amazon, Shipt, Gridwise, Openforce, and Member Benefits are documented partners.[^25][^3]

Stride's strategic wedge is the regulated-stipend administrator role. As of 2025-07-15: DoorDash launched in Pennsylvania (July 2024), Shipt in Utah (July 2024), Lyft in Utah (April 2025), DoorDash in Georgia (April 2025), DoorDash in Maryland (July 2025), and Uber + Lyft in Massachusetts (July 2025).[^2] The Massachusetts program is the first to pool driver hours across multiple platforms — the result of a July 2024 settlement agreement between Uber, Lyft, and the Massachusetts Attorney General's Office.[^3] Stride's platform handles hour-pooling, qualification verification, stipend distribution, and health-plan enrollment.[^3] DoorDash's Maryland pilot, for example, deposits 4% of pre-tip earnings monthly for Dashers who earn at least USD 1,000 from April through June 2025 and complete at least 100 deliveries from Maryland merchants.[^26]

The criticism of these stipends is real. Laura Padin, director of work structures at the National Employment Law Project, testified that DoorDash's 4% contribution amounts to approximately USD 31 a month for most drivers — "not a meaningful contribution to the types of emergencies like disability or sickness that true benefits are intended to cover."[^16] But the regulatory anchor — Stride's role as the named administrator in state-sanctioned pilots — is a structural moat. Stride's first-financial-contributions milestone was July 2024.[^2] By July 2025 the company had touched six state pilots in twelve months.[^2]

### Archetype 3: Captive-insurance pool (Solo Health Vault Captive)

Solo Health Collective is administered by Healthy Business Group through Vault Health Captive, a captive insurance company domiciled and regulated by the North Carolina Department of Insurance.[^6][^7] The structural innovation is regulatory: Solo members are not buying individual ACA insurance; they are establishing self-funded health plans for their "business of one" and joining the captive as class members.[^6] All participating businesses combine funds into a larger pool from which all participants draw. Vault Health Holdings is the captive manager; Vault Admin Services is the plan Administrator; Fairos negotiates provider pricing; Odyssey Reinsurance backs claims that exceed the target loss ratio with no specific or aggregate paid-claim limit.[^6][^8]

Plan design options are V2500, V5000, and V10000 — deductibles of USD 2,500, USD 5,000, or USD 10,000, where the deductible equals the out-of-pocket maximum (with the exception of co-pays for tier 2+ pharmacy benefits).[^6][^7] Network access is the PHCS Multiplan Physician PPO with all-50-state coverage plus emergency-abroad coverage.[^7] The V2500 and V5000 plans are HSA-eligible.[^7] Members pay tax-deductibly: the structure converts what would be a personal premium into a deductible business expense because the captive contracts with the business entity, not the individual.[^27]

Solo's strategic wedge is twofold. First, it is the only major portable-benefits platform that bypasses the ACA marketplace entirely — relevant in a 2026 environment where ACA Marketplace premiums have risen by more than 80% since launch and increased ~7% from 2024 to 2025.[^17] Second, it is the only platform that explicitly markets to "healthy freelancers" through a health questionnaire used to control captive-pool risk.[^28] Solo's 2024 partnership with Freelancers Union widened distribution.[^28] The structural question for Solo is captive scale: captives need enough lives to absorb adverse selection without the reinsurance kicker, and Solo does not publicly report enrollment.

### Archetype 4: Employment cooperative (Opolis)

Opolis (the Employment Commons) is a Colorado-registered Limited Cooperative Association that serves as Employer of Record for solopreneurs operating under their own S-corp or C-corp.[^9][^11] The structural primitive is W-2 conversion: members are co-employed by Opolis as W-2 employees, which qualifies them for group-rated health, dental, vision, retirement, unemployment, and workers' comp insurance.[^11] The fee is 1% on payroll and benefit consumption, plus a one-time USD 20 share purchase to become a member.[^11]

Opolis's group health insurance is Cigna-administered through Cigna's National PPO network.[^29] Plan options as of 2025 included Premium Co-Pay (USD 859.44/month employee-only), Value Co-Pay (USD 420.69/month), Premium HDHP (USD 590.29/month), and Value HDHP (USD 390.97/month).[^29] Members report 20–50% savings versus state ACA exchanges, primarily because Opolis is age-agnostic and does not upcharge premiums by age band.[^30] The cooperative is also the only major platform that integrates Web3 primitives: members can opt into payroll in BTC, ETH, DAI, USDC, or USDT, and the $WORK token grants voting rights and pro-rata profit distribution.[^11][^31]

The structural moat is the W-2 conversion. Solo, Stride, and Catch all preserve 1099 status; Opolis does not. The downstream consequence is access to coverage that 1099 status legally precludes — group health rates, unemployment insurance, workers' comp.[^11] The structural question for Opolis is volume: cooperatives accrue benefits proportional to scale, and Opolis has not publicly disclosed membership counts since 2023.

## The state safe-harbor patchwork

Four state portable-benefits laws have passed and one is pending governor signature.

Utah passed the first state portable-benefits law in 2023, allowing companies to offer portable disability, unemployment, or health benefits to independent contractors without triggering employment status.[^17][^16] Tennessee passed the Voluntary Portable Benefit Plan Act, signed into law April 3, 2025, becoming the second state.[^3][^16] Alabama enacted similar legislation in April 2025.[^16] Wisconsin's bill passed the legislature in June 2025 and is awaiting the governor's signature as of mid-2025.[^2] California's Proposition 22 (2020) is a related but narrower predecessor — it allows gig-economy companies to provide drivers limited benefits including accident insurance and a health-care stipend in exchange for an exemption from the state's ABC employment-classification test.[^17]

Federal legislation has been introduced in both chambers. The Modern Worker Security Act was introduced in the House in February 2025.[^3][^16] The Unlocking Benefits for Independent Workers Act was introduced in the Senate in July 2025 by Senator Bill Cassidy.[^17][^16] Both would create a national safe harbor allowing companies to contribute to worker benefits without triggering employment-status uncertainty under the Fair Labor Standards Act.[^16] The Senate HELP Committee published a 2025-04-16 white paper outlining the policy case.[^17] Critics including Senator Bernie Sanders and the National Employment Law Project's Laura Padin have argued that current portable-benefits proposals "make it easier for large corporations to misclassify workers" and provide insufficient contributions (USD 31 a month from the DoorDash 4% example) to substitute for true insurance-based benefits.[^16]

## The decision matrix

The four archetypes do not compete head-to-head — they compete archetype-against-archetype only at the boundary of the worker's choice. The choice cascades:

| Worker's situation | Best archetype | Why |
|---|---|---|
| Earning irregular 1099 income, wants ACA-subsidy-aware brokerage | Consumer-direct (Catch) | Catch's federal-marketplace standing + tax-withholding integration |
| Earning income from a single gig platform that has a state-sanctioned stipend | B2B2C middleware (Stride) | Stride is the named administrator; no other vendor has the state contract |
| Healthy, established solopreneur, wants ACA-bypass + tax-deductible business expense | Captive (Solo Vault) | The captive structure converts premiums to deductible business expense and bypasses ACA premium inflation |
| Single-owner S-corp or C-corp, wants group-rated coverage + unemployment insurance | Cooperative EOR (Opolis) | Only platform that converts to W-2 status and unlocks group rates + UI |

The vendors themselves do not see this matrix neatly. Stride is expanding into savings and retirement.[^32] Catch has been planning a Series B for several years that has not closed.[^4] Solo is expanding into broader Solo Health Collective products beyond the captive.[^28] Opolis is pursuing on-chain reinsurance bonds.[^33] But the underlying structural alignment is durable: each archetype has a single best-fit worker profile and competes for everyone else only on the margin.

## What this paper does not cover

This paper does not analyze international portable-benefits regimes (the EU Platform Work Directive, Australia's gig-worker reforms, Singapore's CPF for gig workers) — that is a separate paper. It does not benchmark vendor unit economics — none of the four archetypes publishes per-worker contribution-margin or LTV data. It does not analyze adjacent vendors that occupy partial archetypes — Trupo (disability for freelancers), Honest Dollar (retirement for freelancers, acquired by Goldman Sachs in 2016), or PEO platforms like Justworks and TriNet that serve W-2 small-team employers rather than 1099 individuals.

It also does not analyze the post-passage scenario for the federal Modern Worker Security Act or Unlocking Benefits for Independent Workers Act.[^17][^19] Both bills are pre-markup as of mid-2025 and a 2026–2027 vote is plausible but not certain.[^17] The state-level safe-harbor patchwork is the binding constraint until federal preemption arrives.[^16]

## Strategic implications for founders

The four archetypes have different defensibility profiles. Stride's moat is the regulated-administrator role inside state stipend programs — replicable only by being chosen at the state-government table, which is a high barrier. Catch's moat is its federal-marketplace registration (one of three consumer-direct sellers) and its enterprise partnerships, both of which are erodible. Solo's moat is the captive structure and the North Carolina insurance-department regulatory standing — durable but capacity-bounded by the captive's reinsurance terms. Opolis's moat is the cooperative legal structure and the EOR conversion — durable but slow to scale.

The structural opportunity for new entrants is in the regulated-stipend lane that Stride is currently sole-sourcing. As Maryland, Massachusetts, Pennsylvania, Utah, and Georgia bring portable-benefits stipends online, and as more states pass safe-harbor laws, the number of administrator slots multiplies — and Stride does not have an exclusive on any of them. The opportunity for incumbents (Stride, Catch, Solo, Opolis) is the cross-archetype expansion: each is structurally constrained from doing what the others do (Stride cannot easily underwrite, Solo cannot easily broker, Opolis cannot easily serve 1099 status workers), so vertical-slice strategies are likely to dominate horizontal land-grabs.

The 60-million-American TAM is real, and the USD 7.86-billion-by-2033 number is plausible.[^12][^17] What is less plausible is that any single vendor captures more than 25% of it. The market will fragment along the four archetypes — and the strategic question for the next cohort of founders is which archetype is least well-served today. The captive lane (Solo Vault) is the most under-built; the cooperative lane (Opolis) is the most under-funded; the consumer-direct lane (Catch) is the most exposed to enterprise-distribution pressure; the B2B2C lane (Stride) is the most defensible but the most capital-intensive.
