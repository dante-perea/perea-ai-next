## Foreword

A new state-level labor framework for independent contractors emerged in three states in fourteen months. **Massachusetts voters approved Question 3** on November 5, 2024 with **53.9%** of the vote, codified as M.G.L. c. 150F[^1][^2][^3]. **California Governor Gavin Newsom signed AB 1340** — the Transportation Network Company (TNC) Drivers Labor Relations Act — on October 3, 2025, with effect January 1, 2026[^4][^5][^6]. **Illinois SB 2906 / HB 4743** — the Illinois Transportation Network Driver Labor Relations Act — was filed on January 20, 2026 by Senator Ram Villivalam (D-Chicago) and Representative Yolonda Morris (D-Chicago)[^7][^8][^9].

The three statutes share a common architecture: **rideshare drivers remain independent contractors** for purposes of federal employment law (avoiding the AB 5 / Prop 22 wars), but become entitled under **state law** to organize, collectively bargain, and reach **sectoral agreements** that bind all covered Transportation Network Companies. The state is the supervisory body — California's **PERB** (Public Employment Relations Board)[^4][^10], Massachusetts's **Department of Labor Relations**[^3][^11], Illinois's **Labor Relations Board**[^7][^9]. The unionizing partners — SEIU 32BJ in Massachusetts; SEIU California in California; SEIU Local 1 + IAM Local 701 in Illinois — are the same labor-movement actors crossing state lines.

The statutes also share two open litigation theaters: **NLRA preemption** under *San Diego Building Trades Council v. Garmon*, 359 U.S. 236 (1959) — does the National Labor Relations Act preempt state authority over conduct "arguably protected or prohibited" by federal law?[^12][^13] — and **Sherman Act antitrust** under *Parker v. Brown* and the state-action immunity doctrine — can a state legislatively confer antitrust immunity on private competitors who coordinate prices through a state-supervised process?[^14][^15] Both questions are unresolved as of May 2026; both will be litigated post-implementation.

This paper maps all three statutes side-by-side, the December 2025 federal injunction against California's adjacent AB 288 (PERB-NLRB jurisdiction-override) as a leading indicator of preemption risk[^16][^17], and the founder + counsel wedges that the next 18 months will create.

## Executive Summary

Five claims this paper defends:

1. **The three-state cohort is the largest expansion of private-sector collective bargaining in U.S. independent-contractor history.** California's **800,000+ rideshare drivers**[^4][^6][^18], Massachusetts's rideshare-driver workforce, and Illinois's **estimated 100,000+ rideshare drivers**[^7][^9][^19] together total ~1 million IC drivers eligible to bargain — roughly equivalent in scale to the entire pre-2024 net annual private-sector union-card-signing volume.

2. **California AB 1340's mechanics are the canonical template.** The unionization process: a TNC driver organization shows **10% support**[^10] among active drivers to PERB → 30 days verification → companies notify drivers within 30 days → organization shows **30% support**[^10] to trigger an election → if it shows **>50% support**[^10], no election is required and the organization is **automatically certified**[^4][^10][^20]. Active drivers = those completing **20+ rides in the prior 6-month period**[^10]. Quarterly TNC list submission to PERB is mandatory[^4][^10]. Sectoral agreements bind covered TNCs representing **at least 80% of industry rideshare volume**[^21] + at least the **two largest covered TNCs**[^4][^21].

3. **Massachusetts Question 3 was the first-in-nation rideshare-driver collective-bargaining law.** Passed November 5, 2024 with **53.9% yes / 46.1% no** (94% precincts reporting at the AP call)[^1][^2]. Codified as M.G.L. c. 150F[^11]. Certification thresholds: **25% of active drivers** signature threshold[^1][^11]. Backed by **SEIU 32BJ** (Roxana Rivera, assistant to the president; Manny Pastreich, 32BJ president) and **the International Association of Machinists**[^1][^2][^22]. As of December 2025, **no union has been certified** as the representative of Massachusetts rideshare drivers — implementation runway has been longer than expected[^11].

4. **Illinois SB 2906 / HB 4743 was structured around the Massachusetts + California templates.** Filed January 20, 2026 by **State Senator Ram Villivalam (D-Chicago)** + **State Representative Yolonda Morris (D-Chicago)**[^7][^9]. The Illinois Drivers Alliance — coalition of **SEIU Local 1** (President Genie Kastrup) + **IAM Local 701** (Sam Cicinelli, Midwest Territory GVP) — anchors the bill[^9][^19]. Uber agreed not to oppose statewide bargaining legislation in **June 2025** in exchange for the Illinois Drivers Alliance scrapping the Chicago Rideshare Living Wage and Safety Ordinance (Ald. Michael Rodriguez)[^7][^23]. Mechanics: **10%** authorization cards → **30%** for certification → **3-5 year** contract terms[^7][^9]. **210-day** bargaining timeline before impasse triggers mediation + binding interest arbitration[^9].

5. **NLRA preemption + Sherman Act antitrust are the unresolved litigation theaters.** Per CDF Labor Law: "AB 1340 was designed to sidestep NLRA preemption by targeting only independent contractors in the app-platform-based ridesharing sector. Future court cases will undoubtedly be filed to test whether AB 1340 can survive NLRA preemption"[^10]. Per Bona Law's antitrust analysis: AB 1340 "authorizes private competitors to collude and then declares their collusion immune from federal scrutiny"[^14][^15]. The **December 26, 2025** preliminary injunction by Judge Troy L. Nunley blocking California's adjacent **AB 288** (PERB-NLRB jurisdiction override) on Garmon-preemption grounds is the leading indicator that the federal courts will scrutinize state-level labor frameworks where federal law has occupied the field[^16][^17].

## Part I — California AB 1340: The Canonical Template

### Statutory Architecture

Assembly Bill 1340 (Wicks, Berman) — Chapter 335, Statutes of 2025 — was signed by Governor Gavin Newsom on October 3, 2025 in Berkeley, with effect January 1, 2026[^4][^5][^6][^24]. It establishes the **Transportation Network Company Drivers Labor Relations Act** in the Business and Professions Code (Cal. Bus. & Prof. Code §§ 7470 et seq.)[^21][^25].

The legislative history is instructive. The bill cleared the California Senate on a **29-10 vote** (September 8, 2025), with Assembly concurrence in Senate amendments at **60-15** the next day[^25]. Earlier amendments were processed through Senate Labor, Public Employment and Retirement (LPE&R), and Transportation Committees in May–June 2025[^25]. Uber and Lyft initially opposed the bill but **dropped their opposition on August 29, 2025** in exchange for legislators supporting **SB 371**, which reduced the rideshare-industry uninsured/underinsured motorist coverage requirement from **$1 million to $60,000 per individual / $300,000 per accident**[^26][^27].

### Three-Threshold Unionization Mechanics

CDF Labor Law's analysis lays out the unique California unionization process[^10]:

1. **Active driver definition (mandatory quarterly TNC submission to PERB)**. Starting January 1, 2026, covered TNCs must submit to PERB **every quarter** a list of drivers who completed at least **20 rides in the past six months**, with each driver's name, license number, contact information, date joined, and ride count[^10][^21]. PERB calculates the **median ride count** and identifies all "active" drivers — those at or above the median over the prior six-month period[^10].

2. **The 10% Threshold (Initial Recognition)**. A TNC driver organization can begin the union-organization process by making a showing to PERB that it has **10% support**[^10][^21] among active drivers. PERB has **30 days** to verify the threshold is met[^10][^21].

3. **The 30% Threshold (Election Trigger)**. If verified, TNCs must notify active drivers within **30 days** that the union is seeking to represent them. Only after **10% certification**[^10] can PERB provide the union with the list of eligible TNC drivers. The organization then must show **30% support**[^10][^21] to trigger an election. The election is conducted via **remote voting**[^10].

4. **The 50% Automatic Certification**. If the driver organization can show that **more than 50%** of active drivers support it, **no election is required** and the organization is automatically certified as the bargaining representative[^10][^21].

### The Sectoral Agreement Requirement

A "sectoral agreement" is the California statute's defining feature. Under Cal. Bus. & Prof. Code § 7470.13, a sectoral agreement must be approved by **covered TNCs representing at least 80%**[^21] of the industry by rideshare volume on the most recent quarterly report filed with PERB, **and** must include **at least the two largest covered TNCs**[^21]. Mandatory subjects of the sectoral agreement include driver compensation, driver deactivations, paid leave, earnings, and dispute resolution[^4][^28]. PERB approves or disapproves the sectoral agreement after submission[^21].

A TNC that was not a covered TNC when a sectoral agreement took effect, but subsequently becomes a covered TNC, **is bound by all terms** of the sectoral agreement[^21].

### Bargaining-Representative Restrictions

AB 1340 restricts the organizations that may be certified to represent drivers to those that have **experience negotiating a labor contract** or that are **affiliated with such a union**[^29]. Supporters cited this as ensuring legitimate organizations have the resources to represent the bargaining bloc; critics — including Rideshare Drivers United, an organization with **20,000+ California gig driver members** — argued the conditions could unduly benefit SEIU[^29].

### What AB 1340 Does Not Cover

- **Right to strike is not guaranteed.** Per attorney Scott Kronland of Altshuler Berzon (who advised SEIU on AB 1340), the state cannot guarantee the right to strike because of federal antitrust laws[^29][^30].
- **No employee status conferred.** Drivers remain **independent contractors** under Prop 22[^4][^10][^21]. The Castellanos vs. State of California (2024) decision reaffirmed Prop 22's IC classification[^25].
- **Delivery drivers excluded.** AB 1340 does not apply to drivers for delivery apps like DoorDash or Instacart[^6][^29].
- **Drivers under 20 rides per six months excluded** from "active driver" status — meaning purely casual freelance drivers cannot vote[^10].

## Part II — Massachusetts Question 3: The Ballot-Measure Precedent

Massachusetts voters became the **first in the nation** to allow rideshare drivers to unionize on November 5, 2024, when **Question 3** passed with 53.9%–54.1% of the vote (with 94–95% of precincts reporting at the AP call)[^1][^2][^31][^32][^22].

### Statutory Path

Question 3 was enacted as M.G.L. c. 150F. The Massachusetts Department of Labor Relations administers the bargaining process — analogous to California's PERB but using existing state machinery[^11]. The statute provides[^11]:

- **Active driver definition**: drivers who have completed at least the **median number of rides in the last six months**.
- **Certification path 1 (signature)**: a union presents evidence to the Department that at least **25%**[^1][^11] of Active Drivers have designated it as the bargaining representative.
- **Certification path 2 (election)**: the Department conducts a **secret ballot election**; majority of voting active drivers determines outcome.
- **Contract ratification**: drivers who completed at least **100 trips in the most recent quarter** must vote on the proposed agreement.
- **Approval**: the **Massachusetts Secretary of Labor** approves or disapproves the final agreement[^1].
- **Renewal cycle**: every **3 years** (per the original measure language)[^1].

### The 32BJ + IAM Coalition

The campaign was anchored by **SEIU 32BJ** (Roxana Rivera, Assistant to the President; Manny Pastreich, 32BJ President) and the **International Association of Machinists**[^1][^2][^22][^32]. Massachusetts AG **Andrea Campbell** endorsed Question 3[^32][^33]. The Massachusetts AFL-CIO remained neutral due to internal labor-movement concerns about whether IC unionization would slow the path to full employee status[^32].

The political calculus for Uber and Lyft was different from California. The companies had previously settled with AG Campbell — **withdrawing their own ballot question** that would have codified IC status — and **chose not to campaign against Question 3**[^33][^34]. Uber's spokesperson Freddie Goldstein noted the question "just squeaked by" by 53% in a "deeply blue, pro-union state"[^32][^33].

### The Implementation Runway

As of December 2025, **the Massachusetts Department has not certified any rideshare union** as the representative of rideshare drivers. The **App Drivers Union (ADU)** filed a petition with the Department[^11], but no union has yet been certified through either the signature or the secret-ballot path. The runway from ballot passage (Nov 2024) to actual certification has exceeded 13 months — the canonical signal that ballot-driven sectoral bargaining requires significant post-passage operational lift.

## Part III — Illinois SB 2906 / HB 4743: The Third State

### The Coalition

The Illinois Drivers Alliance — a coalition powered by **SEIU Local 1** (President Genie Kastrup) and **IAM Local 701** (Sam Cicinelli, Midwest Territory General Vice President) — is the political anchor of Illinois's bid[^9][^35][^19]. The coalition built grassroots momentum through years of organizing in Chicago, including the Chicago Gig Alliance + Chicago Rideshare Living Wage and Safety Ordinance (Ald. Michael Rodriguez, 22nd Ward, scrapped in June 2025 in exchange for Uber's no-opposition pledge on the state bill)[^7][^23].

### The June 2025 Uber Concession

On June 16, 2025, Ald. Rodriguez scrapped a committee vote on the city ordinance — which would have raised driver pay — in exchange for **Uber agreeing to support state legislation that paves the way for drivers to unionize** + **agreeing not to oppose statewide bargaining legislation** anchored by SEIU Local 1 + IAM Local 701[^23]. Uber had warned the city ordinance would force the company to lay off **10,000 drivers** in Chicago[^23]. The deal was announced hours before the City Council vote[^23].

### The Bill Itself

SB 2906 was filed in the Illinois Senate on January 20, 2026 by Sen. Ram Villivalam (D-Chicago); HB 4743 was filed simultaneously in the House by Rep. Yolonda Morris (D-Chicago)[^7][^8][^9]. The bills create the **Illinois Transportation Network Driver Labor Relations Act**, drawing inspiration from the Massachusetts ballot-measure framework[^7][^9][^35].

**Coverage**[^9][^36]:
- Active driver definition: 90 days after enactment, all TNCs must provide the Illinois Labor Relations Board with a list of drivers completing **5+ rides in the previous six months**. From this list, drivers with at or above the **median rides** are "active."
- "Covered TNC" = any TNC the Board determines to represent a combined **95%** of all TNC rides in the state quarter[^36].

**Mechanics**[^9][^36]:
- 10% **signed authorization cards** from active drivers across all covered TNCs to access the full driver list.
- 30% from full driver list for certification as exclusive statewide bargaining representative.
- 210-day bargaining period before impasse triggers **mediation** and, if necessary, **binding interest arbitration**.
- Sectoral agreement applies to covered TNCs representing **at least 95% of the rideshare volume**[^36].
- Ratification by drivers who completed **100+ trips in the previous calendar quarter**, simple majority.
- After approval by the **Illinois Department of Labor**, agreement is binding for **3-5 years**[^36].

### The Funding Mechanism Controversy

A 20-cent per ride **administrative fee** on riders is the bill's funding mechanism. Per testimony from **Kimberly Stevens**, Executive Director of the Illinois Labor Relations Board on March 11, 2026, the fee structure presents a **conflict of interest**: "That would come to the board to then create and administer grants back to the union out of that funding, which presents a conflict of interest for an agency that is supposed to be neutral"[^37]. Stevens estimated the bill would **double the agency's budget**, cost **$3-5M to stand up systems**, and require **at least 50% headcount increase**[^37].

## Part IV — The NLRA Preemption Open Question

The defining federal-law challenge to the three-state cohort is **NLRA preemption**. The relevant doctrine — *San Diego Building Trades Council v. Garmon*, 359 U.S. 236 (1959) — holds that "[w]hen an activity is arguably subject to §7 or §8 of the [NLRA], the States as well as the federal courts must defer to the exclusive competence of the National Labor Relations Board"[^12][^25]. As Justice Barrett restated in *Glacier Northwest, Inc. v. International Brotherhood of Teamsters Local Union No. 174*, 598 U.S. 771 (2023), "Preemption under the NLRA is unusual, though, because our precedent maintains that the NLRA preempts state law even when the two only arguably conflict"[^25].

### The IC-Carve-Out Theory

The defensive theory underlying all three statutes: **independent contractors are excluded from coverage under the NLRA** (29 U.S.C. § 152), so they do not have federal collective-bargaining rights, and therefore state legislation to provide such rights is not "arguably" subject to §7 or §8 of the NLRA[^25]. CDF Labor Law summarizes: "AB 1340 was designed to sidestep NLRA preemption by targeting only independent contractors in the app-platform-based ridesharing sector"[^10].

### The Drivers-Are-Actually-Employees Counter-Theory

The vulnerability: if a court finds that rideshare drivers are misclassified — that they are in substance employees rather than independent contractors — then the NLRA's exclusion of ICs no longer protects the state framework, and Garmon preemption potentially kicks in. California's Castellanos v. State of California (2024) decision reaffirmed Prop 22's IC classification under state law[^25] — but that ruling does not bind a federal court applying federal labor law.

### The AB 288 Federal Injunction (December 26, 2025)

The leading indicator of how federal courts will scrutinize state labor-law expansion is California's adjacent **AB 288**, signed by Newsom September 30, 2025 and **enjoined by U.S. District Judge Troy L. Nunley on December 26, 2025** in NLRB v. California[^16][^17][^38][^39]. AB 288 authorized PERB to take on certain private-sector cases where the NLRB lacked a quorum, failed to act within prescribed time limits, or otherwise declined jurisdiction[^16][^39].

Judge Nunley held the NLRB was likely to succeed on its preemption claim because AB 288 "grants PERB authority over conduct that is subject to regulation by the NLRA, and allows PERB to 'stand in the way of the NLRB's administration of the NLRA'"[^16]. He noted AB 288's provisions concerning loss-of-quorum and processing-delay-based jurisdictional cessation conflict with the NLRA, which "anticipates that the NLRB might lose a quorum" and provides for delegation of powers to the Board's administrative functions[^16].

The NLRB had filed suit on October 15, 2025[^16][^39]; the Trump administration's DOJ intervened in support[^17]. New York's Section 715 of its State Labor Relations Act — a parallel "NLRB Trigger Bill" — was similarly enjoined on November 26, 2025[^16][^17].

The ICLG analysis frames it: "Barring a reversal by the Ninth Circuit, Judge Nunley's order may mark the end of California's attempt to expand PERB's powers into the NLRB's traditional jurisdiction... private-sector employers can expect to remain subject to the NLRB's jurisdiction for the foreseeable future"[^17].

The cohort-spanning implication: the **AB 288 + NY § 715 + Connecticut State Labor Relations Act** template — state agencies asserting jurisdiction over NLRA-covered conduct — has been firmly rebuffed at the preliminary-injunction stage. The **AB 1340 + Q3 + IL SB 2906 framework** sits in a structurally different position because it covers IC-classified drivers (who are NLRA-excluded) — but the same federal-supremacy logic provides the litigation roadmap.

## Part V — The Sherman Act Antitrust Open Question

Beyond NLRA preemption, AB 1340 (and by structural parallel, the Massachusetts and Illinois statutes) faces **Sherman Act antitrust scrutiny**. The argument, articulated by Aaron Gott of Bona Law, runs as follows[^14][^15]:

**The structural problem**: "A 'union' of independent contractors is, in the eyes of federal law, just another word for a cartel. Without some form of immunity, sectoral bargaining among gig drivers would expose everyone involved in this horizontal cartel to **treble damages and potential criminal liability** under § 1 of the Sherman Act"[^14].

**The state-action immunity doctrine**: *Parker v. Brown*, 317 U.S. 341 (1943), and subsequent cases (most recently *NC Dental Examiners* (2015)) establish that states can confer antitrust immunity on private parties **only if** (a) the conduct is undertaken pursuant to a "clearly articulated and affirmatively expressed" state policy, AND (b) the state actively supervises the substantive content of the conduct[^15].

**Why AB 1340 likely fails**: AB 1340 declares that "the state-action antitrust exemption shall apply"[^15]. But per Bona Law, this fails the **active-supervision** prong: "PERB's role is to certify, mediate, and formalize collective agreements. Nothing in the statute directs the Board to analyze whether an agreement advances state policy or to assess its competitive effects... The Board's authority is procedural, not substantive"[^14].

**The federal preemption pathway**: Under *Rice v. Norman Williams Co.* and *California Retail Liquor Dealers v. Costco*, "facial preemption is appropriate when a state statute compels or authorizes per se unlawful conduct"[^15]. Bona Law's analysis: AB 1340 (1) mandates a collective-bargaining process among independent contractors; (2) authorizes them to coordinate prices and output; (3) declares their resulting conduct lawful and exempt from the Sherman Act — and therefore is "more vulnerable than the Seattle ordinance" struck down by the Ninth Circuit in *Chamber v. Seattle*[^15][^40].

**The right-to-strike caveat**: Even AB 1340's drafters acknowledged the antitrust constraint. The statute does not guarantee the right to strike[^29][^30] — Scott Kronland of Altshuler Berzon told KQED that "the state can't guarantee the right to strike because of federal antitrust laws"[^29].

The litigation pathway: per Bona Law, "A pre-enforcement declaratory judgment under 28 U.S.C. §§ 2201 and 1331 could resolve the issue before the law takes effect"[^15] — meaning a federal-court antitrust challenge could move forward immediately upon AB 1340's January 1, 2026 effective date.

## Part VI — Founder + Counsel Wedges

Six wedges remain open in 2026–2027, ordered by defensibility:

### 1. The Three-State Sectoral-Bargaining Compliance Suite

Each state's compliance requirements differ on critical mechanics: California's **20-ride active-driver threshold + 10/30/50 unionization cascade**[^10], Massachusetts's **median-rides + 25% signature path + 100-trip ratification**[^11], Illinois's **5-ride active threshold + 30% certification + 210-day bargaining period**[^9]. A SaaS that ingests a TNC's driver-activity logs, computes per-state active-driver rosters, manages the quarterly state-board submissions, and tracks bargaining-process events captures the compliance perimeter for 800K + 100K + Massachusetts drivers spanning three jurisdictions.

### 2. The NLRA Preemption Litigation Tracker

Federal-court rulings on AB 1340, Q3, and SB 2906 will arrive in 2026–2027. A specialty practice + tracker that maintains the full litigation docket, pre-classified by Garmon-preemption + antitrust vectors, with Cherokee-warden-style alert triggers for new filings, captures the labor-and-employment litigation segment that is currently underserved relative to its commercial value.

### 3. SaaS for the Sectoral Agreement Audit Trail

A sectoral agreement under AB 1340 must be approved by covered TNCs representing **80%**[^21]+ of rideshare volume + the two largest covered TNCs[^21]. A specialty SaaS that audits ride-volume reports, certifies the **80% + top-2** condition, and produces the PERB submission package captures a recurring 3-year cycle. Same product applies in Illinois (**95% threshold**[^36]) + Massachusetts (Department of Labor Relations approval).

### 4. The Driver-Side App for Active-Driver-Status Self-Assessment

The 20-rides / median-rides / 5-rides active-driver definitions are operationally complex from the driver's perspective. A driver-side mobile app that ingests the driver's Uber/Lyft trip log via official APIs, computes per-state active-driver status, and surfaces unionization-vote eligibility captures the long-tail solo-driver cohort that the SEIU + IAM organizers cannot canvass individually.

### 5. The 4th-State Statutory-Drafting Lane (NY, MN, WA, NJ)

Minnesota drivers were already cited as "pushing for similar rights" in October 2025 KCRA coverage[^41]. New York, Washington, and New Jersey have rideshare-driver-organizing momentum. A specialty law practice + legislative-drafting consultancy positioned to stand up the next 2-3 state frameworks (drawing from the AB 1340 / Q3 / IL SB 2906 templates) captures the statutory-design billable hour for the 2027 session.

### 6. Cross-State Sectoral-Agreement Harmonization

Once California, Illinois, and (eventually) Massachusetts each have certified bargaining representatives and operating sectoral agreements, the cross-state harmonization problem becomes acute. A driver working in CA, OR, WA must navigate three state frameworks; a TNC must manage three sets of sectoral agreement obligations. A SaaS or consultancy that maps cross-state harmonization (via reciprocity provisions, common minimum-pay floors, etc.) captures the multistate operational layer.

## Part VII — Open Questions and What This Paper Does Not Cover

Five threads this paper leaves open:

1. **Minnesota and the next-state cohort.** Minnesota drivers have been cited as pushing for similar rights[^41]; New York, Washington, and New Jersey are the most likely 4th-state candidates for the 2026–2027 sessions. The state-by-state sequencing is unmapped.
2. **The actual ratification timeline.** Massachusetts ballot passage in November 2024 has not yet produced a certified union by December 2025. The 13+ month implementation runway suggests California and Illinois will take similar or longer paths. The future paper should track milestones.
3. **The first sectoral-agreement contract terms.** The fully bargained sectoral agreement under AB 1340 will be the first IC-cohort sectoral agreement in U.S. private-sector history. The contract terms (minimum pay, deactivation appeal rights, paid leave, expense reimbursement, vehicle-cost allocation) will set the national template.
4. **The Sherman Act litigation outcome.** Bona Law's pre-enforcement-declaratory-judgment thesis has not yet been tested in court. The federal-court ruling on AB 1340's antitrust state-action immunity defense will arrive in late 2026 or 2027.
5. **The NLRA-amendment political pathway.** The PRO Act and other federal labor reform proposals would extend NLRA coverage to certain IC categories — moot the state-level workaround. The 2026 election cycle and the subsequent legislative session is the relevant decision point.

## Glossary

- **Sectoral agreement** — under AB 1340, an agreement between covered TNCs and the certified driver bargaining organization that, if approved, applies to all covered TNCs. Must cover ≥80% of industry by rideshare volume + the two largest covered TNCs[^21].
- **Active driver** — an IC rideshare driver meeting state-specific ride-count thresholds (20+ in 6 months for California; median rides for Massachusetts; median from 5+ list for Illinois)[^9][^10][^11].
- **TNC (Transportation Network Company)** — a company operating in a state that uses an app to connect drivers (using personal vehicles) with passengers; Uber, Lyft, etc.[^4][^10].
- **PERB (Public Employment Relations Board)** — California state agency administering AB 1340, including driver-list collection, certification, election supervision, and sectoral-agreement approval[^4][^10][^21].
- **Department of Labor Relations** — Massachusetts state agency administering Question 3 / M.G.L. c. 150F[^11].
- **Illinois Labor Relations Board** — Illinois state agency that would administer SB 2906 / HB 4743 if enacted; concerns raised about budget doubling + 50% headcount increase[^37].
- **Garmon preemption** — under *San Diego Building Trades Council v. Garmon*, 359 U.S. 236 (1959), states cannot regulate conduct "arguably protected or prohibited" by §7 or §8 of the NLRA[^12][^25].
- **State-action immunity (Parker doctrine)** — under *Parker v. Brown*, 317 U.S. 341 (1943), and its progeny, states can confer antitrust immunity on private parties only if (a) acting pursuant to clearly articulated state policy and (b) actively supervising substantive conduct[^14][^15].
- **NLRA Section 7 / Section 8** — sections of the National Labor Relations Act protecting concerted-activity rights of "employees" (29 U.S.C. § 152), excluding independent contractors.
- **Prop 22** — California ballot measure (November 3, 2020) classifying app-based drivers as independent contractors, codified as Cal. Bus. & Prof. Code §§ 7448-7467; upheld in Castellanos v. State of California (2024)[^25].
- **AB 288** — separate California bill signed September 30, 2025, authorizing PERB to take on private-sector cases when the NLRB ceded jurisdiction; preliminarily enjoined by Judge Troy L. Nunley on December 26, 2025[^16][^17].
- **M.G.L. c. 150F** — Massachusetts General Laws chapter codifying Question 3's rideshare-driver collective-bargaining framework[^11].
- **SB 371** — California companion bill reducing TNC uninsured/underinsured motorist coverage requirements from $1M to $60K/$300K, signed alongside AB 1340 as part of the August 2025 deal[^26][^27].
- **App Drivers Union (ADU)** — union that filed a petition with the Massachusetts Department of Labor Relations to represent rideshare drivers under M.G.L. c. 150F[^11].
- **Castellanos v. State of California (2024)** — California Supreme Court decision unanimously upholding Prop 22's IC classification of app-based drivers[^25].
