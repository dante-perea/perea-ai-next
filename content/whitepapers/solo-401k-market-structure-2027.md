## Foreword

The Solo 401(k) is the highest-contribution-limit retirement vehicle the IRS lets a one-person business buy, and for two decades it lived in a quiet backwater behind the SEP-IRA and the SIMPLE IRA. That changed in 2024–2025. SECURE 2.0 Act tax credits made employer-sponsored plans cheap to start[^1][^2]. State retirement-plan mandates spread to 20 states[^3]. Cerulli Associates published a forecast saying micro-401(k) plans (under $5M in assets) will hit **more than 1 million plans by the end of the decade** — a 36% increase over five years and 66% over the 600,000-plan baseline at end-2023[^4][^5]. JP Morgan Asset Management entered the Solo 401(k) market on July 16, 2025[^6]. ForUsAll crossed $1 billion in advised assets[^7]. The brokerage tier (Fidelity, Schwab, Vanguard, E*TRADE) standardized on $0 setup and $0 annual fees while quietly differentiating on Roth and loan provisions[^8]. The self-directed tier (Rocket Dollar, MySolo401k) carved out a $750-$1,500 setup, $299-$1,000/year niche for crypto, real estate, and Mega Backdoor Roth[^9].

This paper maps the entire structure: the regulatory backbone, the vendor cohort tiered by economics, the SECURE 2.0 + Form 8881 + Form 5500-EZ compliance perimeter, and the founder wedges still open as the micro-plan market triples in headcount this decade.

## Executive Summary

Five claims this paper defends:

1. **Cerulli's 1M-plan forecast is the structural number.** The U.S. Retirement Markets 2024 report[^4] projects micro-401(k) plans will exceed[^4] 1 million by 2029 — a[^4] **36% increase over five years**, building on the 600,000-plan baseline at end-2023[^4][^5][^10]. By 2029, Cerulli expects **92% of all 401(k) plans** to sit in the micro segment, a near-40% increase from 2022[^11].

2. **JP Morgan's July 2025 entry validates the segment for incumbents.** JPMorgan Chase launched Solo 401(k) on July 16, 2025 as an expansion of Everyday 401(k), with **Vestwell as recordkeeper**[^6][^12]. Pricing: **$540 base fee/year**, **$350 setup** for new plans, **15bps asset-based** (declining to 7bps at $1M+), **$150/yr** for a spouse account[^13]. JP Morgan markets to a population of **7M+ Chase for Business customers**[^6].

3. **The vendor cohort splits into three tiers by economics.** **Brokerage tier** (Fidelity Self-Employed 401(k), Charles Schwab Individual 401(k), E*TRADE Solo 401(k), Vanguard Individual 401(k)): $0 setup, $0 annual, $0 stock/ETF trades, but most omit Roth or loan provisions[^8][^14]. **Bundled-recordkeeper tier** (JP Morgan Solo 401(k) via Vestwell, ForUsAll, Carry, Guideline): $0–$540 setup, $6–$45/month plus 0.0417–0.5%/month asset fees, but Roth + loans + automation included[^13][^15]. **Self-directed tier** (Rocket Dollar, MySolo401k.net, IRA Financial): **$525–$1,500 setup**, **$125–$1,000/year**, with **alternative assets, real estate, crypto, Mega Backdoor Roth**[^16][^17][^9].

4. **SECURE 2.0 + Form 8881 made the credit math undeniable.** The IRS Form 8881 (Rev. December 2025) lets employers with 1–50 employees claim the **greater of $500 or $250 × number of eligible employees** (capped at $5,000 per year) for plan startup costs, plus a **separate employer contribution credit** of up to $1,000 per employee[^1]. SECURE 2.0 tax incentives were the single most-cited reason cited by recordkeepers and wealth advisors for new-plan adoption in 2023–2025 surveys[^2][^18][^19].

5. **The founder wedges are state-policy + payroll-integration.** Of the **20 states** that had enacted state-facilitated retirement savings programs as of January 2025, **15 of which had existed for fewer than five years** had accumulated slightly more than **$2 billion in assets** by May 2025[^2][^20]. PEP (pooled employer plan) assets were on track to approach **$25 billion by year-end 2025**, up from $9.4 billion in 2023[^2][^21]. Fidelity's PEP business grew **117% year-over-year** in plan assets from 2023 to 2024[^2]. The unfilled niche is the payroll-integrated, state-mandate-aware Solo 401(k) onboarding flow that converts a sole-proprietor 1099 into a plan participant in under five minutes.

## Part I — The Macro Setup: Cerulli, SECURE 2.0, and the State Mandates

The Cerulli Associates *U.S. Retirement Markets 2024* report (published January 9, 2025) is the clearest forward look at where this market is going[^4]. Three numbers anchor it:

- **>1 million micro-plans by ~2029.** From 600,000 at end-2023[^5], growing at[^4] ~36% over five years[^5][^4][^10]. The Cerulli Edge — U.S. Retirement Edition, 2Q 2025 Issue (published July 2025) reaffirmed the trajectory[^11].
- **92% of all 401(k) plans in the micro segment by 2029** (those with under $5 million in assets), a near-40% increase from 2022[^11].
- **150,000 new 401(k) plans added between 2018 and 2023**, with nearly two-thirds in 2021 and 2023[^11] — the two years immediately after the original SECURE Act and as SECURE 2.0 was being negotiated.

The drivers are three:

**SECURE 2.0 Act of 2022 tax incentives.** The IRS Form 8881 (Rev. December 2025) tabulates the credit with arithmetic precision[^1]: employers with 1–50 employees enter qualified startup costs on Line 1, then `Line 3 = number of eligible employees × $250`, then `Line 4 = greater of $500 or Line 3` (capped at $5,000), then `Line 5 = smaller of Line 2 or Line 4` is the credit[^1]. There is a separate **employer contributions credit** in Section 2 of the form covering up to $1,000 per non-highly-compensated employee for the first two years, declining to 75%, 50%, and 25% in years 3–5[^1]. Brian Williams of Northshire Consulting told *PlanSponsor* in August 2025 that SECURE 2.0 tax incentives "are successfully incentivizing employers to start new plans"[^2]. Marc Fowler, director of retirement education at Human Interest, reported plan adoption among firms with 51–100 employees at **1.7× the prior period rate** since SECURE 2.0's January 2023 implementation, and a **3× rate** among firms under 50 employees[^2].

**State retirement-plan mandates.** As of January 2025, **20 states** had enacted state-facilitated retirement savings programs for private employers without their own plans, and **15 of those 20** had existed for under five years[^2][^20]. Their combined assets had crossed **$2 billion** by May 2025 — small versus the[^2] **$12.2 trillion** total in U.S. defined contribution plans[^2], but enough to push small businesses into adopting their own plans rather than enrolling in the state default[^2]. Brian Williams's framing: "If a small business is using the [SECURE 2.0 Act] tax credit to create a well-designed, low-cost, efficient plan, they know they have a competitive advantage against an employer using a high-fee, low-service, state-run IRA"[^2].

**Pooled Employer Plan (PEP) growth.** The Georgetown University Center[^2] for Retirement Initiatives estimated PEP assets would approach[^2] **$25 billion by year-end 2025**, up from[^2] **$17 billion in 2024** and **$9.4 billion in 2023**[^2][^21]. Fidelity Advantage 401(k) (Fidelity's PEP) grew **117% year-over-year** in plan assets 2023–2024[^2]. Principal's EASE PEP saw nearly **70% of participating employers** new to offering a retirement plan, with **three-quarters under 100 participants**[^2][^22].

A critical interaction: SECURE 2.0 retroactively clarified that joining an existing MEP or PEP qualifies as "establishing a plan" for tax-credit purposes regardless of how long the pooled arrangement existed[^2]. This collapsed a gating ambiguity that had kept PEP economics weaker than they should have been.

## Part II — The Solo 401(k) Itself: Structure, Limits, and Filing

The Solo 401(k) is technically a "one-participant" 401(k). It exists for self-employed individuals or business owners with **no full-time employees other than a spouse**[^23][^24]. Critical mechanics every founder should know:

**Contribution architecture.** A Solo 401(k) accepts both employee elective deferrals (subject to the universal 401(k) limit) and employer profit-sharing contributions[^24]. For 2026:

- **Employee elective deferral**: up to $24,500 (or 100% of compensation, whichever is less)[^13].
- **Catch-up (age 50+)**: additional $8,000, totaling $32,500[^13].
- **Catch-up (ages 60–63, SECURE 2.0 enhanced catch-up)**: additional $11,250, totaling $35,750[^13].
- **Employer profit-sharing**: up to 25% of compensation for incorporated businesses (W-2 wages) or **approximately 20% of net self-employment income** for unincorporated businesses[^13][^24].
- **Total contribution limit**: $72,000 (2026), $79,500 if age 50+, $83,250 ages 60–63[^13][^25].

The Solo 401(k) holder also runs a "spouse-employed-by-the-business" account: the spouse can contribute the same employee + employer amounts, doubling household tax-advantaged savings to **$144,000+**[^6][^26]. JP Morgan markets this directly: "small business owners can save up to $70,000 annually... and up to an additional $70,000 for their spouse, if employed by the business"[^6][^26].

**Form 5500-EZ filing.** Annual filing is **not required until plan assets exceed $250,000** as of December 31 of the previous year[^27][^28][^29]. Once over the threshold, the plan files Form 5500-EZ on paper with the IRS — Rocket Dollar's documentation notes that 5500-EZ "must be physically mailed," so providers offering Form 5500-SF as the electronic alternative typically charge **$150–$500** for filing assistance[^9][^28].

**Loan provisions** are a sharp differentiator. IRC §72(p)(2)(B)[^29] caps the loan at[^29] **50% of vested balance or $50,000, whichever is less**[^29], requires substantially level amortization with payments at least quarterly, and demands a five-year payoff (15 years for primary-residence loans)[^29]. Rocket Dollar's specification of "prime rate +1 or +2%" as a reasonable interest rate is the practitioner standard[^29]. Among the brokerage tier: Fidelity, Schwab, and Vanguard's standard Solo 401(k) plan documents **do not allow loans**[^8][^30][^14]. E*TRADE does[^14]. The self-directed Solo 401(k) market is built around the loan provision: Rocket Dollar markets it as "you are the bank and pay yourself back principal and interest"[^29].

**Roth contributions.** Available at JP Morgan Solo 401(k) (Vestwell)[^6], ForUsAll[^15], E*TRADE[^14], and the self-directed tier (Rocket Dollar, MySolo401k)[^29][^17]. Vanguard added Roth to their Individual 401(k) prior to offloading the small-business retirement division to Ascensus[^25]. Fidelity's Self-Employed 401(k) historically did not support Roth contributions in its prototype document, though policy has shifted[^31].

**Mega Backdoor Roth.** The strategy of contributing post-tax dollars beyond the elective-deferral limit and converting them in-plan to Roth, requires a custom plan document — available at MySolo401k.net (~$525 setup + $125/yr)[^17] but not at the standard brokerage tier[^25].

**ERISA Title I exemption.** A one-participant plan is exempt from ERISA Title I's fiduciary, reporting, and disclosure requirements[^32]. Adding even one non-spouse W-2 employee voids the Solo status — and SECURE 2.0's part-time eligibility provision (employees working 500 hours over two consecutive years must be allowed to participate) shortens the runway before that happens[^2].

## Part III — The Vendor Cohort, Tiered

### The Brokerage Tier: $0 / $0 / $0

**Fidelity Self-Employed 401(k)** — $0 setup, $0 annual fee, $0 stock/ETF commissions, access to **3,300+ no-transaction-fee mutual funds** including the Fidelity ZERO Index Fund family (FZROX, FZILX) at **0% expense ratio**[^31][^33][^14]. Investopedia's *Best Solo 401(k) Companies for May 2026* analysis (which evaluated **14 providers across 21 weighted criteria**, with data collected March 23–26, 2025) named Fidelity the best overall provider[^14]. The historical limitation was no Roth and no loans on the prototype plan; Fidelity has been narrowing both gaps[^31].

**Charles Schwab Individual 401(k)** — $0 setup, $0 annual, $0 stock/ETF trades, **4,200+ no-load no-fee mutual funds**, 24/7 customer service, no Roth and no loans on the prototype plan[^14][^30]. Schwab's competitive advantage is service depth and the brokerage's banking integration via Schwab Bank.

**Vanguard Individual 401(k)** — Vanguard's strength was the lowest expense ratios in the industry[^33]: average mutual fund ER[^33] **0.10% versus 0.63% industry average**[^33], average ETF ER **0.06% versus 0.25% industry average**[^33]. The historical fee was $20 per Vanguard fund per year, waived once a participant held $50,000+ across all Vanguard accounts[^33]. **Vanguard offloaded its small-business retirement division to Ascensus**, which introduced a new fee structure that prompted SoloWealthLab and bestsolo401k.com to recommend Fidelity or Schwab over the new Ascensus offering[^25][^34].

**E*TRADE Solo 401(k)** — $0 setup, $0 annual, $0 stock/ETF, with **Roth deferrals** and **plan loans** included on the standard prototype plan[^14][^30]. SoloWealthLab's *Complete Solo 401(k) Guide for Freelancers (2026)* and SelfEmployed.com's *6 Best Low-Cost Solo 401(k) Plans for Self-Employed (2026)* both ranked E*TRADE #1 for the combination of Roth + loans without annual fees[^25][^16].

### The Bundled-Recordkeeper Tier: $0–$540 setup, $6–$45/month + asset-based fees

**JP Morgan Asset Management Solo 401(k)** — Launched July 16, 2025[^6][^12]. Recordkeeper: **Vestwell Holdings, Inc.**[^6][^26]. Pricing: **$540/year base fee**, **$350 setup fee** for new plans (or $500–$0 conversion fee depending on prior plan size), **15bps asset-based fee** below $500K, declining to **10bps from $500K–$1M** and **7bps over $1M**, plus **$150/year** for a spouse account[^13]. Steve Rubino, Head of Retirement at JP Morgan Asset Management, called it "a significant milestone in our commitment to enhance retirement planning for solo entrepreneurs"[^6]. John McCourt, Chase's head of client relationships, frames the population: "Today's economy is powered by self-starters — consultants, creators, contractors — who have redefined what it means to work"[^6][^35]. Chase for Business serves **7M+ businesses**[^6]. Investor reaction was concrete: JPM's share price rose **23% in the quarter following the launch**, with **$7.1 billion in share buybacks** layered atop the product news[^36].

**ForUsAll Solo 401(k)** — Built in partnership with Opolis (a self-employment-cooperative DAO that runs the "Employment Commons")[^37][^38]. Pricing: **$6/month** flat plus **0.0417%/month** (0.50%/year) on account balance, deducted quarterly[^15]. **Roth + loans + crypto window included**, the latter being unique among 2026 Solo 401(k) providers[^15]. ForUsAll crossed **$1 billion in advised assets** in early 2026, supporting **350 businesses and 60,000 employees**[^7]. Maximum contribution: **$70,000/year** ($77,500 ages 50–59, $81,250 ages 60–63)[^15]. The crypto window — direct exposure to digital assets inside the 401(k) — sits inside ForUsAll's plan documents with ERISA fiduciary coverage handled by ForUsAll as **3(38) Investment Fiduciary** plus **3(16) Administrative Fiduciary**[^15]. The Opolis partnership is the channel: Opolis members get the plan at **$6/month for the Employment Commons membership tier**[^38][^39].

**Vestwell Solo(k)** — Vestwell offers the recordkeeping platform itself directly to advisors and self-employed clients independent of the JP Morgan partnership, advertising **integrated recordkeeping, 3(16) fiduciary services, contribution tracking, and optional payroll sync**[^26]. The marketing positioning: "Unlike SEP or SIMPLE IRAs, Vestwell's Solo(k) can be seamlessly amended into a traditional 401(k) plan as your client hires employees"[^26] — directly addressing the SECURE 2.0 part-time-eligibility risk that voids Solo status.

**Guideline** — Owned by Gusto since the May 2024 acquisition; per industry coverage[^57] Gusto is valued at **$9.3B** with Guideline supporting[^57] **1M+ savers across 65,000 businesses**[^57]. Guideline pairs Solo 401(k) eligibility checks with payroll deductions automatically.

**Carry Corporation** — A self-employment-financial-stack vendor whose September 2025 research found that of **9.7 million unincorporated self-employed Americans** participating in retirement savings, **only 21% have established a Solo 401(k)** — pointing directly at the conversion gap that the cohort is racing to capture[^40].

### The Self-Directed Tier: $525–$1,500 setup, $125–$1,000/year

**Rocket Dollar Self-Directed Solo 401(k)** — Core: **$360 setup + $15/month**; Gold: **$600 setup + $30/month**[^16][^9]. Gold removes custodian sign-off requirements, includes IRA Trust checking with optional LLC, and waives AUM fees[^14]. The product is built around **alternative assets**: real estate (with checkbook control), private placements, hard money lending, precious metals, and crypto — all of which require a custom plan document and trust structure[^41][^42][^29][^9]. **Loans** are the unique enabling feature: up to **50% of plan value or $50,000**, whichever is less[^29]. UDFI exemption is a frequently cited tax advantage: the Solo 401(k) is exempt from Unrelated Debt-Financed Income tax, unlike a Self-Directed IRA, when using leverage to buy real estate[^42].

**MySolo401k.net** — Roughly **$525 setup + $125/year** for plan-document drafting and ongoing administration[^16][^17]. The Mega Backdoor Roth Solo 401(k) is the differentiating feature: contributing post-tax dollars beyond the elective-deferral limit and converting them in-plan to Roth, accessible only with a custom plan document[^17].

**IRA Financial** — Publishes a 2026 *Solo 401(k) Provider Fees Explained* guide that maps the entire fee landscape: setup $0–$1,500, annual $299–$1,000, **Form 5500-EZ filing $150–$500**, **loan setup $75–$250 + servicing $25–$100/year**[^9]. The full self-directed Solo 401(k) economic profile they cite: setup $750–$1,500, annual $299–$1,000, 5500-EZ $150–$500, loan setup $100 + $50/year ongoing, Roth support included or small add-on[^9].

**Merrill Edge Solo 401(k)** — Hybrid pricing: **$100 one-time setup**, **$20–$25/month** business fee depending on assets, **$3/month recordkeeping fee per individual**, and a **0.52% annual asset fee** covering customer support and custodial services[^14][^31]. The product is unusual in that it offers loans + Roth on the prototype, but the all-in cost is materially higher than Fidelity/Schwab.

## Part IV — Adversarial Layer: Hidden Costs and the 1.96% Debate

The MarketWatch advisor-fee debate from 2024–2025[^43] named the[^43] **1.96% advisor fee** as the cited average cost when a wealth advisor sells a Solo 401(k) plan as part of a broader financial-planning relationship — versus the **0%–0.5% all-in cost** of a direct-to-consumer brokerage Solo 401(k)[^43]. Across **14 Solo 401(k) providers** Investopedia analyzed in March 2025, the cost variance between the cheapest and the most expensive at $500,000 in assets approached **$10,000/year**[^14].

The hidden costs surface in three places:

**Advisor share classes.** "Adviser-sold" plans, per Cerulli, accounted for **50–99% of plans sold in 2023** at **57% of DC recordkeepers**[^4][^5]. The advisor receives 12b-1 trail commissions plus periodic plan-review fees. Cerulli's structural take: "the growth of the micro 401(k) market will further the ongoing convergence of the wealth management and DC plan markets"[^5][^11].

**Form 5500-EZ filing fees.** Once plan assets cross $250,000, the annual filing requirement kicks in[^28][^29]. Self-directed providers charge **$150–$500/year** for filing assistance[^9]. Brokerage-tier plans typically expect the participant to file directly — a non-trivial compliance burden for a sole proprietor.

**Plan-loan administrative fees.** The $50,000-or-50% loan provision[^29] is the Solo 401(k)'s unique feature, but loan administration is not free[^9]: **setup $75–$250, annual servicing $25–$100/year, modification or payoff processing fees** at the self-directed tier[^9]. The IRS imputed-distribution rules under IRC §72(p) make a missed payment expensive, and the substantially-level-amortization-with-quarterly-payments standard is a real operational discipline[^29].

The structural tell: **JP Morgan's Solo 401(k) at $540/year + 7–15bps asset-based + $150 spouse fee is cheaper than the bundled-recordkeeper average for plans over ~$200K** (where the asset-based fee compresses below 15bps), and competitive with the brokerage tier when accounting for the advisor + Roth + loan + automation features included[^13].

## Part V — The Workforce Backdrop: 9.7M, 21%, and the Conversion Gap

Carry Corporation's September 2025 research[^40] established the population denominator: **9.7 million unincorporated self-employed Americans** participate in retirement savings[^40]. **Only 21% of them have established a Solo 401(k)**[^40]. The remainder default into Traditional or Roth IRAs (capped at $7,000–$8,000/year for 2026), SEP-IRAs (no Roth, no loans, no employee contribution), or no retirement vehicle at all.

This is the conversion gap. Bridging it requires solving for:

1. **The elective-deferral confusion.** Sole proprietors[^25] must compute their employer contribution as[^25] **20% of net adjusted self-employment income** (net business profit minus half the SE tax)[^25][^14]. SoloWealthLab's worked example: hitting the $72,000 cap requires roughly $190,000 in W-2 salary for an S-corp owner or **$250,000 in net profit for a sole proprietor**[^25].
2. **The Form 5500-EZ trigger.** The $250,000 asset threshold creates a friction step that providers either solve for (self-directed tier with bundled filing) or push to the participant (brokerage tier).
3. **The state-mandate scoping.** A sole proprietor in Illinois, California, Oregon, Colorado, Maine, Connecticut, and the other 14 mandate states has to either enroll in the state default or open a private-plan alternative — and SECURE 2.0 tax credits make the private alternative cheaper than it has been in two decades[^2].
4. **The payroll integration.** The fastest-growing segment of the bundled-recordkeeper tier (JP Morgan via Vestwell, ForUsAll, Guideline) ties contribution timing to payroll, eliminating the December-31 deferral deadline cliff[^26].
5. **The spouse-account doubler.** A married couple operating jointly can stack $144,000+ in tax-advantaged retirement savings annually — a cap that is, per the Cerulli + Carry data, the single largest under-utilized tax shelter for high-income freelancers[^6][^40].

The marketing message that the cohort consistently lands on: "Solo 401(k)s offer one of the highest IRS contribution limits"[^14], "save more for retirement than almost any other plan"[^26], "your ticket to a financially secure future"[^38].

## Part VI — Founder Wedges

Six wedges remain open in 2026–2027, ordered by defensibility:

### 1. Payroll-integrated, state-mandate-aware Solo 401(k) onboarding

Today's onboarding flow is asynchronous: the sole proprietor selects a provider, fills out paper plan documents, opens a trust bank account, and waits 1–4 weeks. The wedge is a five-minute, payroll-API-integrated onboarding that pulls 1099 / Schedule C data from the user's accounting software (Lettuce, Found, Collective, Bonsai, Hurdlr), computes the optimal employee + employer contribution, and creates the plan document, EIN, trust, and brokerage account in a single flow. Vestwell's existing payroll-sync architecture is the closest extant primitive[^26].

### 2. The CCBHC-equivalent "PEP for Solos" play

PEP assets are growing at 117% YoY (Fidelity Advantage 401(k))[^2] but no PEP today is purpose-built for the **9.7M unincorporated self-employed**. A Pooled Employer Plan structure designed for one-person businesses — sharing fiduciary, administrative, and Form 5500 filing across thousands of solos — would compress the per-plan cost from $540/year (JP Morgan baseline) to under $100/year, mirroring the Colorado Access measurement-based-care wedge in behavioral health.

### 3. Mega Backdoor Roth for the post-$250K saver

The Mega Backdoor Roth — contributing post-tax beyond the elective-deferral cap and converting in-plan — sits today only at MySolo401k.net (~$525 setup + $125/yr)[^17]. A mainstream brokerage offering it at $0 setup with automated tracking would unlock an additional **~$30,000/year of Roth space** for sole proprietors above the elective-deferral cap. The constraint is plan-document complexity, not regulatory: the IRS allows it for any plan with the right document.

### 4. Crypto + alternative-asset 401(k) at retail pricing

The ForUsAll + Opolis crypto-window flow is the canonical example[^15][^38]; Rocket Dollar covers the broader alternative-asset stack at $360+/year[^16]. The gap is pricing alternative-asset access at brokerage-tier economics: $0–$50/year, not $360–$600/year. The constraint is custody + custodian fees, not regulation.

### 5. The 5500-EZ-as-a-service product

Once plan assets cross $250,000, Form 5500-EZ filing becomes a perpetual annual obligation[^28][^29]. Today's options are: pay your self-directed provider $150–$500[^9], pay a CPA $200–$600, or file directly. A specialty SaaS at $49/year that pulls plan assets from the brokerage API, generates the Form 5500-EZ PDF, and walks the participant through paper-mailing it to the IRS captures the long-tail of self-administered plans approaching the threshold.

### 6. The "Solo to Traditional Conversion" product

SECURE 2.0's part-time eligibility provision (500 hours × 2 years) is a ticking clock[^2]: every Solo 401(k) holder who hires even one part-time worker faces forced conversion to a multi-participant plan within ~26 months. Vestwell's "amend to traditional 401(k)" framing[^26] is the right pattern but needs productization at the bundled-recordkeeper tier as a one-click event with automated non-discrimination testing, safe-harbor election, and HR onboarding flow.

## Part VII — Open Questions and What This Paper Does Not Cover

This paper is a survey, not a comparison benchmark. Five threads it leaves open:

1. **The Vanguard-to-Ascensus transition consequence.** The market reaction to Vanguard's offload of small-business retirement to Ascensus shifted recommendations against Vanguard at SoloWealthLab, bestsolo401k.com, and others[^25][^34]. The aggregate AUM impact and Ascensus's competitive economics deserve a dedicated paper.
2. **The MarketWatch[^43] 1.96% advisor-fee debate.** The fee debate is well-documented but the actual cross-vendor advisor-fee distribution is not[^43]. A future paper should triangulate.
3. **State-mandate compliance economics.** 20 states, 15 of which under five years old, with $2B in state-default assets[^2][^20] — the comparative economics of state default vs private Solo 401(k) for a sole proprietor at different income brackets is unmapped.
4. **The crypto window 2026 IRS posture.** The IRS Department of Labor 2022 cautionary guidance on crypto in 401(k) plans never matured to formal regulation; ForUsAll's crypto window remains in a regulatory gray zone[^15].
5. **SECURE 2.0 part-time eligibility implementation.** The 500h × 2-year provision implementation guidance is incomplete; the IRS Notice covering technical mechanics has been deferred[^2].

## Glossary

- **Solo 401(k)** (also "individual 401(k)", "i401(k)", "Solo-k", "one-participant 401(k)"): a 401(k) plan for a self-employed individual with no full-time employees other than a spouse[^23][^24].
- **Form 8881**: IRS form filed by employers to claim the small-employer pension plan startup costs credit (capped at $5,000/year) plus the employer contributions credit (up to $1,000 per employee for years 1–2)[^1].
- **Form 5500-EZ**: IRS form filed annually for one-participant plans with assets above $250,000 (paper filing only)[^28][^29].
- **Form 5500-SF**: electronic version of Form 5500 for smaller multi-participant plans; some self-directed Solo 401(k) providers file Form 5500-SF on behalf of clients with under $250K in assets[^28].
- **SECURE 2.0 Act of 2022**: federal legislation expanding tax incentives for small-business retirement plans, raising catch-up contribution limits, and creating the part-time eligibility (500h × 2y) requirement[^2][^18].
- **PEP (Pooled Employer Plan)**: multi-employer plan structure that pools fiduciary and administrative responsibilities across multiple unrelated employers[^2][^21].
- **MEP (Multiple Employer Plan)**: similar to PEP but typically with a common nexus among the participating employers[^2].
- **CC PPS** (Solo 401(k) version): not applicable; Solo 401(k) plans use a clinic-style PPS only in the broader CCBHC context — Solo 401(k) economics use direct-fee pricing instead.
- **Mega Backdoor Roth Solo 401(k)**: strategy of contributing post-tax dollars beyond the elective-deferral limit and converting them in-plan to Roth; requires a custom plan document[^17].
- **3(38) / 3(16) fiduciary**: ERISA fiduciary roles for investment selection (3(38)) and plan administration (3(16))[^15][^26].
- **UDFI (Unrelated Debt-Financed Income)**: tax on debt-financed income inside a self-directed retirement account; Solo 401(k) is exempt from UDFI on real-estate leverage, unlike Self-Directed IRAs[^42].
- **ERISA Title I exemption**: one-participant plans are exempt from ERISA Title I fiduciary, reporting, and disclosure requirements[^32].
- **SECURE 2.0 part-time eligibility**: provision requiring employees with 500+ hours over two consecutive years to be offered participation, voiding Solo 401(k) status when triggered[^2].
- **Vestwell**: independent recordkeeping platform powering JP Morgan's Everyday 401(k) + Solo 401(k), plus other advisor-channel offerings[^6][^26].
