A new fiduciary-process safe harbor is being constructed for the $11+ trillion U.S. defined-contribution retirement market. **Executive Order 14330**, signed August 7, 2025 and published August 12, 2025 at 90 Fed. Reg. 38921[^1][^2][^3], directed the Department of Labor to "democratize access to alternative assets for 401(k) investors" — private equity, private credit, real estate, digital assets, commodities, infrastructure, and lifetime-income strategies — within 180 days. **On March 31, 2026, the DOL's Employee Benefits Security Administration published the implementing notice of proposed rulemaking** at 91 Fed. Reg. 16088 (RIN 1210-AC38), proposing a process-based safe harbor at 29 CFR §2550.404a-6 with six fiduciary factors[^4][^5][^6]. Public comments are due **June 1, 2026**[^7]. This paper maps the regulatory text, the supply-chain partnerships now in flight, the SEC's parallel accredited-investor and qualified-purchaser revisions, and the Schlichter / Phalippou / Tobe litigation-and-economics critique that will define the next two years of ERISA litigation.

## Executive Summary

1. **Executive Order 14330 is a 180-day directive, not a rule change in itself.** Trump signed EO 14330 on **August 7, 2025**, published as 90 FR 38921 on August 12, 2025, citing more than **90 million Americans** participating in employer-sponsored defined-contribution plans largely without alternative-asset access[^1][^8]. The order defines six "alternative asset" categories: (i) private market investments (private equity + private debt), (ii) real estate, (iii) actively managed digital-asset vehicles (crypto), (iv) commodities, (v) infrastructure projects, (vi) lifetime-income strategies including longevity risk-sharing pools[^2][^9]. The EO directed the Secretary of Labor — within 180 days — to (a) reexamine ERISA fiduciary-duty guidance, including potential rescission of the December 21, 2021 Supplemental Private Equity Statement, (b) propose rules with "appropriately calibrated safe harbors," and (c) prioritize actions that curb ERISA litigation constraining fiduciaries' best judgment[^1][^2][^9].

2. **The March 31, 2026 NPRM (RIN 1210-AC38) is the operative document.** Published at **91 Fed. Reg. 16088–16144** (57 pages)[^4], the proposal would amend 29 CFR Part 2550 to add §2550.404a-6, a **six-factor process-based safe harbor** for fiduciaries selecting designated investment alternatives (DIAs) under participant-directed defined-contribution plans[^5][^10]. The six factors (drawn from Section 3(c) of EO 14330): **performance, fees, liquidity, valuation, performance benchmarks, and complexity**. A fiduciary who follows the prescribed process for a factor receives a **presumption of prudence** and "significant deference" with respect to that factor[^11][^12]. Comments are due **June 1, 2026**[^7].

3. **The proposed rule applies to all DIAs, not just alternatives.** The DOL deliberately structured the safe harbor as asset-class-neutral — citing the December 2021 Biden statement's "potentially costly chilling effect" — and the safe harbor is therefore available for any DIA other than brokerage windows or self-directed brokerage accounts[^4][^13]. As Deputy Secretary of Labor Keith Sonderling stated, "the department's days of picking winners and losers are over"[^7].

4. **The supply chain has moved faster than the regulator.** Even before the NPRM, **Empower** — second-largest U.S. retirement-plan provider serving more than **19 million plan participants** — launched a private-markets program on May 14, 2025 with seven initial partners (Apollo, Franklin Templeton, Goldman Sachs, Neuberger Berman, PIMCO, Partners Group, Sagard)[^14][^15]. **Blackstone joined Empower's program on January 14, 2026** with $1.2 trillion in assets across PE, credit, real estate, infrastructure[^16][^17]. **OneDigital announced January 20, 2026** that it was partnering with Blackstone, Apollo, and Ares Management for adviser-managed portfolios[^18]. **Apollo + Schroders** announced a US DC-market collective-investment-trust launch for Q2 2026 on February 9, 2026[^19]. Goldman Sachs's **Private Credit CIT** sits inside Great Gray's Panorix Target Date Series with a BlackRock custom glidepath that allocates **5–20% to private markets**[^20][^21][^22].

5. **The CIT structure is the regulatory pivot point.** **Collective investment trusts now hold 54%**[^23] of all target-date assets ($4.8T[^24] total target-date market end-2025; Vanguard alone holds $1.8T[^24] / 37%[^24] market share)[^23][^24]. CITs sit outside the Investment Company Act of 1940's accredited-investor and qualified-purchaser limits because the **plan itself** (rather than each participant) qualifies as the accredited investor or qualified purchaser[^25]. The SEC has historically permitted a defined-contribution plan to count as a single accredited investor when conditions are met — meaning the path of least resistance for private-market access is **through CIT-wrapped target-date funds**, not direct fund participation by individual savers[^26][^25].

6. **The SEC's parallel track is reopening accredited-investor and qualified-purchaser definitions.** On February 11, 2026, SEC Chair Paul Atkins testified before House Financial Services that the agency is **"actively collaborating"** with EBSA and that the accredited-investor and qualified-purchaser definitions "need addressing and updating"[^27][^28]. The current accredited-investor thresholds — **$1 million net worth**[^29] (excluding primary residence) or **$200,000 / $300,000 income**[^29] — were set by Regulation D in 1982 and have never been indexed; **$5 million in investments**[^29] is the qualified-purchaser threshold under Investment Company Act §2(a)(51)[^29]. SEC ADI 2025-16 (August 2025) had already relaxed the informal "15% rule" for closed-end funds-of-private-funds, accepting structural protections instead[^29].

7. **Tibble's ongoing-duty rule is the unresolved contradiction.** *Tibble v. Edison International* (135 S. Ct. 1823, 2015) held that a fiduciary's duty of prudence is **continuing** — failure to remove an imprudent investment within ERISA's six-year limitations window restarts the clock[^30]. The DOL's safe harbor protects *selection*, but Tibble protects *monitoring*; the harder question is whether a fiduciary who selected a private-equity DIA prudently must subsequently monitor the GP's bespoke fee structure, side-letter terms, and offshore domicile through a 7–12-year lockup[^31][^32]. Critics (Schlichter, Phalippou, Tobe) argue that PE's all-in costs at **roughly 600 basis points** annually, vs. **3–10 basis points** for index funds, cannot satisfy ERISA's reasonable-fee requirement, and that GP-controlled valuations preclude meaningful Tibble-style monitoring[^31][^32].

8. **Daniel Aronowitz's DOL — formerly president of Encore Fiduciary, which sells trustee insurance — has vowed to "end the era of regulation by litigation."** The safe harbor is the operative weapon: it is intended to give plan fiduciaries a documented process that pre-empts complaints at the motion-to-dismiss stage[^33][^34]. Encore Fiduciary tracked **roughly 450 fee or forfeiture lawsuits**[^33] filed from 2020 through September 2025, the vast majority targeting plans with **more than $500 million**[^33] in assets — a tiny fraction of the **roughly 800,000 401(k) plans**[^33] in the U.S. but enough to drive plan-sponsor caution[^33].

## Regulatory Architecture: From EO 14330 to 29 CFR §2550.404a-6

### The Executive Order — what it directed and what it did not

Executive Order 14330 was signed by President Trump on **August 7, 2025** and published in the Federal Register on August 12, 2025[^1][^2]. The order's preamble cited "more than 90 million Americans" in employer-sponsored DC plans without access to "alternative asset" investments routinely held by public pension plans[^1][^9]. The order's policy declaration (Section 2) stated that "every American preparing for retirement should have access to funds that include investments in alternative assets when the relevant plan fiduciary determines that such access provides an appropriate opportunity for plan participants and beneficiaries to enhance the net risk-adjusted returns on their retirement assets"[^1][^9].

Section 3 directed three specific actions within 180 days[^1][^9]:

- **3(b)**: The Secretary of Labor shall reexamine DOL guidance on fiduciary duties under ERISA in connection with offering an asset-allocation fund that includes alternative assets, and **consider whether to rescind the DOL's December 21, 2021 Supplemental Private Equity Statement** (the Biden-era cautionary statement that flagged PE inclusion absent DB-pension fiduciary experience).
- **3(c)**: The Secretary shall propose rules, regulations, or guidance — "which may include appropriately calibrated safe harbors" — clarifying fiduciary duty when offering asset allocation funds with alternative-asset components, and shall "prioritize actions that may curb ERISA litigation that constrains fiduciaries' ability to apply their best judgment"[^1][^2].
- **3(e)**: The SEC, in consultation with DOL, shall consider revisions to existing regulations and guidance — "including, but not limited to, consideration of revisions to existing SEC regulations and guidance relating to accredited investor and qualified purchaser status"[^1][^2][^9].

Critically, the EO **did not change the law** — it directed agency action[^3][^35]. As Kirkland & Ellis's August 2025 alert noted, the EO builds on a June 3, 2020 first-Trump-administration DOL Information Letter (the Breyfogle letter) that had stated a professionally managed asset-allocation fund with a PE component could be consistent with ERISA fiduciary duties[^3][^36].

### The March 31, 2026 NPRM — the safe-harbor framework

On **March 30, 2026**, EBSA announced a "historic proposed regulation"[^7]; the formal Federal Register publication was **March 31, 2026 at 91 Fed. Reg. 16088**, document number 2026-06178, RIN 1210-AC38[^4]. The NPRM is **57 pages** (16088–16144) and proposes to add **29 CFR §2550.404a-6** to the existing 1979 Investment Duties regulation at §2550.404a-1[^4][^11].

Paragraph (f) of the proposed §2550.404a-6 introduces the **process-based safe harbor**, with six non-exhaustive factors detailed in paragraphs (g) through (l)[^4]:

1. **Performance** (paragraph (g))**.** The fiduciary must consider a reasonable number of similar investment alternatives and determine that the DIA's risk-adjusted expected returns, **net of fees**, over an appropriate time horizon, further the plan's purpose[^11][^37]. The NPRM specifically provides that an "appropriate time horizon for retirement savings may be long-term" — a textual anchor for accepting alternatives' multi-year hold periods[^11].

2. **Fees and Expenses** (paragraph (h))**.** The fiduciary must determine that fees and expenses are appropriate in light of risk-adjusted expected returns and any other value (lifetime income, diversification). **Fiduciaries are not required to select the lowest-fee alternative**[^37][^11]. This is the single most consequential provision relative to the *Tibble* fee-litigation architecture.

3. **Liquidity** (paragraph (i))**.** The DIA must have sufficient liquidity to meet plan needs at both the **plan level** (terminations, recordkeeper changes) and the **participant level** (retirement, separation, hardship)[^4][^11]. For 1940-Act-registered open-end funds, compliance with **Rule 22e-4** (liquidity risk management program) automatically satisfies this factor[^11]. For non-registered DIAs (CITs and traditional private funds), the fiduciary may rely on a **written representation** from the manager confirming adoption of a substantially equivalent program — provided the fiduciary "reads, critically reviews, and understands" the representation and has no reason to question it[^11]. The NPRM explicitly allows quarterly-redemption products[^38] and acknowledges illiquid assets may produce an "illiquidity premium"[^38].

4. **Valuation** (paragraph (j))**.** For assets without readily observable market prices, a prudent process may rely on a written representation that valuations are produced through a **conflict-free, independent process no less frequently than quarterly**, in accordance with **FASB ASC 820** (Fair Value Measurement) or any successor standard[^11][^39]. For 1940-Act-registered funds, review of audited financial statements and Rule 2a-5 disclosures suffices[^11]. The NPRM **explicitly identifies affiliated or conflicted valuation processes — such as a continuation fund where the manager controls valuation inputs — as failing both the safe harbor and ERISA's underlying prudence standard**[^11][^31].

5. **Performance Benchmarks** (paragraph (k))**.** The DIA must have a "meaningful benchmark" — defined as "an investment, strategy, index, or other comparator that has similar mandates, strategies, objectives, and risks to the [DIA]"[^37]. Fiduciaries may rely on benchmarks created by a prudently selected independent investment-advice fiduciary[^37].

6. **Complexity** (paragraph (l))**.** The fiduciary must determine whether it has the skills, experience, and capacity to understand the investment sufficiently[^37].

The safe harbor applies to **all DIAs other than brokerage windows or self-directed brokerage accounts**[^10][^13]. Treasury Secretary Scott Bessent and SEC Chair Paul Atkins each issued endorsing statements in the EBSA release[^7][^40].

### The political-economy framing inside the rule

Two textual choices in the NPRM are doing significant work[^4][^11][^41]:

- The DOL **deliberately did not limit the safe harbor to alternatives**. The reasoning, stated in the preamble, is that DOL guidance should not appear to favor or disfavor any asset class. The Biden-era December 2021 statement's "departure from neutral and principles-based" guidance is named as the foil. The result: the safe harbor benefits target-date funds, stable value funds, and any other DIA — not just PE/private credit/real estate.
- The DOL pre-empts the **"regulation by litigation"** problem named by EO 14330 Section 3(c). EBSA Assistant Secretary Daniel Aronowitz — formerly president of Encore Fiduciary, which sells fiduciary-liability insurance to plan trustees — has explicitly framed the rule as a litigation shield[^33][^34]. The mechanic: a fiduciary who follows the prescribed process for a factor is presumed prudent on that factor and entitled to "significant deference," giving district courts a textual basis to dismiss complaints at the pleading stage where complaints fail to plead departure from process[^11][^34].

## Supply-Side: Who Is Actually Building the Product

The supply chain pre-dates the rule. Empower — second-largest U.S. retirement-plan provider — launched its private-markets program on **May 14, 2025**, three months before EO 14330 was signed[^14][^15]. Initial managers: Apollo Global Management, Franklin Templeton, Goldman Sachs Asset Management, Neuberger Berman, PIMCO, Partners Group, and Sagard, with a Goldman Sachs product launching subject to internal approvals[^14][^15]. The structure: **collective investment trusts (CITs) accessed through advice-based managed accounts**, not direct insertion into the plan's core investment menu[^14][^17].

The expansion since EO 14330:

- **January 14, 2026**: Empower added **Blackstone** ($1.2T AUM across PE, credit, real estate, infrastructure)[^16][^17][^42]. Blackstone Vice Chairman Jon Gray and Heather von Zuben (global head of retirement solutions) announced the partnership; Empower CEO Edmund Murphy III framed the program as managed-account-based ("through advice and risk-appropriate structures")[^17][^42].
- **January 20, 2026**: **OneDigital** — a major employer-side 401(k) advisor — announced a partnership with Blackstone, Apollo, and Ares Management Corp. to incorporate PE and private credit into adviser-managed portfolios[^18].
- **February 9, 2026**: **Apollo + Schroders** announced a strategic partnership; the U.S. component is a **CIT dedicated to the DC pension market launching Q2 2026**[^19]. Apollo CEO Marc Rowan said he expects Vanguard and Fidelity to become "the largest buyers" of private-market products[^19]. (Apollo + State Street launched the **PRIV** investment-grade private-credit ETF with daily liquidity in February 2025; PRIV held nearly **$700 million in assets** by early 2026[^19].)
- **Q4 2025**: **Goldman Sachs Private Credit CIT** debuted in Great Gray Trust Company's **Panorix Target Date Series**, drawing on Goldman's **$142 billion**[^20] private-credit platform, with sub-advisor Wilshire Advisors managing a liquidity sleeve[^20].
- **Mid-2025**: **BlackRock LifePath Paycheck**, the firm's flagship private-market-integrated TDF, established a **5–20% allocation**[^21] to private markets through a custom glidepath. Participants under 35 receive **20% private-market exposure**[^21] (12% PE / 8% private credit), declining to 5% at retirement age[^21][^43].

A Cerulli Associates survey of nearly 1,000 retirement plan sponsors found **37%**[^17][^44] had a high level of interest in learning about private-market options, and Cerulli projected that by 2030, **7%**[^17][^44] of plan sponsors may have a private-markets allocation through a target-date fund or managed account[^17][^44].

The structural choice — CIT inside a TDF inside a managed-account wrapper — is not incidental. CITs do three things at once: (a) qualify the plan rather than the participant for accredited-investor / qualified-purchaser status[^25], (b) sit outside the Investment Company Act's daily-redemption obligations applicable to mutual funds, (c) preserve fiduciary control over the manager-selection decision (selecting the CIT, not the underlying private fund) that the §2550.404a-6 safe harbor protects.

## The CIT Glidepath Is Eating the Mutual Fund Glidepath

By end-2025, **CITs represented 54%**[^23][^24] of total target-date assets, up from **52%**[^23] the year before; the target-date market grew **20.3% in 2025 to $4.8 trillion**[^23][^24]. Five firms control **80%**[^24] of target-date assets: Vanguard ($1.8T, 37% market share)[^24], Fidelity, T. Rowe Price, BlackRock, Capital Group[^23][^24]. Within CITs specifically, Vanguard holds **38%**[^23], T. Rowe Price 14%, BlackRock 13%, Fidelity 8%[^23].

Twenty-one new CIT series launched through 2025[^23]. The Morningstar 2026 Target-Date Fund Landscape characterizes most as "structural conversions" — institutional CIT versions of existing mutual-fund TDFs — rather than new strategies. The exceptions, explicitly named: products incorporating **income-generating assets, annuities, or private investments** (BlackRock LifePath Paycheck)[^23].

Vanguard remains the notable holdout. The firm has stated that "liquidity transformation risks outweigh return benefits" in DC plans[^21]. Fidelity's countermove uses **direct indexing overlays on public equities to replicate private-market factor exposures**, though backtests show this captures only **63% of the illiquidity premium**[^21].

## SEC Track: Accredited Investor + Qualified Purchaser Reopening

Section 3(e) of EO 14330 directed the SEC to consider revisions to existing regulations and guidance, including but not limited to those relating to **accredited-investor** and **qualified-purchaser** status[^1][^9]. Two months after the EO, SEC ADI 2025-16 (August 2025) **relaxed the informal "15% rule"** — the long-standing limit on closed-end-fund-of-private-funds (CE-FOPFs) investments by non-accredited investors — and replaced it with **structural protections**: SEC-registered adviser, board oversight, periodic disclosures with liability for material misstatements[^29].

On **February 11, 2026**, SEC Chair Paul Atkins testified before House Financial Services that the SEC is "actively collaborating" with EBSA, and that **accredited-investor and qualified-purchaser definitions "need addressing and updating"**[^27][^28]. Atkins specifically called the wealth-and-income line "arbitrary" and floated extending professional-credential pathways (Series 7, 65, 82) into 401(k) plan participants[^27].

The relevant thresholds today[^29]:

- **Accredited investor** (Reg D Rule 501(a), set in 1982 and never indexed): **$1 million net worth**[^29] excluding primary residence, or **$200,000 individual / $300,000 joint income**[^29] for two prior years.
- **Qualified purchaser** (1940 Act §2(a)(51)): **$5 million in investments**[^29] for natural persons; **$25 million in investments**[^29] for entities (subject to look-through rules).
- **Closed-end-fund-of-private-funds 15%**[^29] rule (informal, now relaxed under ADI 2025-16[^29]): formerly restricted CE-FOPFs to accredited investors.

For DC plans specifically, the SEC has historically permitted **a plan to qualify as a single accredited investor or qualified purchaser** (rather than each participant) when conditions are met[^25][^27]. This is the structural reason CITs inside target-date funds are the dominant access channel.

Two pieces of legislation are also in motion: Rep. French Hill (R-Tex.) reintroduced a bill broadening the accredited-investor definition (allowing education, professional experience, or credentials to count), and Rep. Mike Lawler (R-N.Y.) co-sponsored a bill directing the SEC to create a FINRA-administered accredited-investor exam[^45].

## Litigation + Economic Critique

### The Schlichter doctrine — and the safe harbor's response

Jerome Schlichter (Schlichter Bogard LLP) pioneered modern ERISA fee litigation. His most-cited results include the **Anderson v. Intel**[^33] case-line and an early settlement in the ABB 401(k) plan case (**$55 million**[^33], 2019); plaintiffs in the Yale TDF litigation that Schlichter helped design were **paid more than $42 million**[^33] through trial, then a federal jury found largely in Yale's favor in 2023[^33]. **Pentegra Retirement Services agreed in late 2025 to pay $48.5 million**[^33] to resolve excessive-recordkeeping-fee allegations[^33].

According to plan-insurer Encore Fiduciary, **roughly 450**[^33] fee or forfeiture lawsuits were filed from 2020 through September 2025 — the vast majority targeting plans with **more than $500 million**[^33] in assets[^33]. That is a tiny share of the **roughly 800,000 401(k) plans**[^33] in the U.S., but as American Benefits Council SVP Lynn Dudley told Bloomberg, "the less you do, the less likely you are to be sued"[^33]. Schlichter himself, in a February 2026 interview, said: "Their job is going to be a whole lot more difficult to carry out that duty if private credit or private equity is in the plan, because it's opaque, it's quite a bit more expensive, and it's illiquid"[^33].

The safe harbor's mechanic against this litigation pattern: a plan fiduciary who follows the prescribed six-factor process is **presumed prudent** on those factors and entitled to "significant deference"[^11][^34]. District courts now have a textual basis to dismiss complaints at the pleading stage where the complaint fails to plead deviation from process. In *Peeler v. Bayada Home Health Care, Inc.* (W.D.N.C., No. 1:24-cv-00231, January 27, 2026), Judge Reidinger dismissed a traditional excessive-fee suit, holding that comparator plans on Form 5500 were not in fact comparable and that bid-solicitation allegations alone do not state a plausible imprudence claim[^46].

### The Phalippou / Tobe critique

Independent academic research (Ludovic Phalippou, Oxford Saïd) finds that **PE all-in costs exceed roughly 600 basis points annually** — vs. **3–10 bps for index funds** — once carry, management fees, monitoring fees, transaction fees, and financing spreads on affiliated debt are summed[^31]. Chris Tobe (CommonSense 401k Project) argues this creates a per-se ERISA prohibited-transaction problem under **29 U.S.C. §1106(a)** and §1106(b): GP and affiliates are parties in interest, indemnification clauses are textbook self-dealing, side letters transfer value away from ERISA investors, and offshore domiciles + the **25%**[^32] ERISA plan-assets exemption under the DOL plan-asset regulation are deliberate evasions of fiduciary oversight[^32][^31]. Carried interest of **20%+**[^32] of profits plus management fees of **1.5–2%**[^32] plus monitoring/transaction fees plus financing spreads compound to the 600-bps figure[^32].

Tobe's deeper point is structural: **PE contracts typically lock fiduciaries into 7–12-year**[^32] illiquid structures with little or no exit rights, valuations are GP-controlled, and secondary sales are limited and discounted with GP consent required[^32]. Under *Tibble v. Edison International*, 575 U.S. 523 (2015), the fiduciary duty of prudence is **continuing** — the failure to monitor and remove an imprudent investment restarts the six-year limitations clock[^30]. A 7–12-year lockup with GP-controlled valuations arguably makes Tibble-compliant monitoring impossible[^31][^32].

The DOL safe harbor addresses this in three specific places:

- **Valuation** (paragraph (j)): explicitly identifies affiliated/conflicted valuation processes — **including continuation funds where the manager controls valuation inputs** — as failing both the safe harbor and ERISA's underlying prudence standard[^11][^31].
- **Liquidity** (paragraph (i)): allows **quarterly redemption** structures and acknowledges the illiquidity premium, but requires the fiduciary to determine sufficient liquidity at both plan and participant levels[^11][^38].
- **Fees** (paragraph (h)): explicitly does **not** require selecting the lowest-fee alternative — pointing fiduciaries toward a value-for-money analysis rather than a mechanical fee comparison[^37][^11].

### *Cunningham v. Cornell* and the burden of proof

The Supreme Court's 2025 decision in *Cunningham v. Cornell University* held that, for ERISA prohibited-transaction claims under §1106, **the burden rests with fiduciaries** to demonstrate an exemption under §1108[^32][^46]. Tobe argues this means a fiduciary placing plan assets into an offshore PE vehicle where 25%+ of the fund is ERISA-plan assets must affirmatively demonstrate satisfaction of an exemption — a burden the structures Tobe describes are designed to evade[^32]. Whether the DOL's process safe harbor will be read by courts as sufficient to satisfy Cunningham's burden is the open litigation question[^32][^46].

## What This Means for Plan Sponsors, Founders, and Counsel

### For plan sponsors

The path is now clearer than it has been since the 2021 Biden statement, but **the safe harbor is process protection, not asset-class endorsement**. The mechanical implications:

1. **Document the six-factor analysis for every DIA**, not just alternatives. The safe harbor extends to all DIAs (except brokerage windows), so the same documentation discipline that mitigates litigation risk for an alternative-asset DIA also strengthens defense of conventional DIA selections[^11][^13].
2. **Get written manager representations on liquidity (Rule 22e-4-equivalent program) and valuation (FASB ASC 820 quarterly)** for non-1940-Act DIAs[^11][^39]. The fiduciary must "read, critically review, and understand" them, and may not know information that contradicts them[^11].
3. **Avoid continuation-fund structures and other manager-controlled valuation patterns** — the NPRM names these as failing the safe harbor[^11].
4. **Document the meaningful-benchmark choice**, including the rationale for any reliance on a third-party investment-advice fiduciary's benchmark[^37].

### For founders

Four wedges:

1. **Compliance / safe-harbor documentation SaaS**. Each plan fiduciary will need a documented record proving objective, thorough, analytical consideration of all six factors for every DIA. A SaaS that ingests fund prospectuses, manager representations, and benchmark choices and produces a defensible §2550.404a-6 record set per DIA per plan year is a recurring annual product. Initial market: ~30,000+ plans with **$50M+**[^33] AUM (lower bound; the Schlichter litigation pattern targets plans above **$500M**[^33]).
2. **Independent valuation infrastructure for non-1940-Act DIAs**. The NPRM requires conflict-free valuations no less frequently than quarterly, in accordance with FASB ASC 820. Independent valuation providers (Duff & Phelps, RSM, BDO) handle the institutional segment but the DC-CIT segment is only beginning to scale. A specialty valuation engine that produces ASC-820-compliant quarterly valuations for CIT-wrapped private-fund holdings, with audit-trail documentation tailored to the DOL safe harbor, is a recurring annual product.
3. **Liquidity-forecasting for the participant-level test**. The §2550.404a-6(i) test requires sufficient liquidity at the participant level (retirement, separation, hardship). A liquidity-engine SaaS that ingests participant demographic data, models cash-flow demands from layoffs / hardship withdrawals / asset reallocations, and produces a daily liquidity-coverage ratio per CIT per plan is the operational layer underneath BlackRock's "liquidity cascade" approach[^21].
4. **Glidepath construction for sub-scale TDF providers**. Vanguard, Fidelity, T. Rowe Price, BlackRock, and Capital Group control 80% of the TDF market[^23][^24]. The remaining 20% is distributed across smaller providers who lack the in-house glidepath research capacity to incorporate alternatives. A glidepath-engine SaaS that produces age-appropriate alternative-asset allocations with documented academic backing is a defensible mid-market wedge.

### For ERISA counsel

The two open questions, both for the comment period and for post-finalization litigation:

- **Does the safe harbor's presumption survive *Cunningham*'s burden-of-proof reallocation?** *Cunningham v. Cornell University* (2025) put the burden on fiduciaries for §1106 prohibited-transaction defenses. The DOL safe harbor is structured around §404(a)(1)(B) prudence, but plaintiffs will plead §1106 alongside §404. Whether documented compliance with §2550.404a-6 establishes a §1108 exemption — or merely satisfies §404 — is unsettled[^32][^46].
- **Does Tibble's continuing-duty rule survive a 7–12-year PE lockup?** The safe harbor protects the *selection* of a DIA. Tibble protects ongoing *monitoring*. Where a CIT holds a PE GP for 10 years and the manager controls valuations, the fiduciary's ability to "remove" an imprudent investment is structurally limited. The comment period (closing **June 1, 2026**) is the venue for raising this question[^7][^31].

## What This Paper Does Not Cover

This paper is an authority survey of EO 14330 and the March 31, 2026 NPRM. It does **not** cover: (a) the parallel SEC rulemaking process to revise the accredited-investor and qualified-purchaser definitions (treated only at the level of Chair Atkins's stated direction), (b) the December 11, 2025 Trump executive order on foreign-owned and politically motivated proxy advisors[^9], (c) state-level retirement program responses to private-market access (CalSavers, OregonSaves, Illinois Secure Choice), (d) the parallel labor-and-tax architecture for IRA private-market access under EO 14330's Section 3(e) referrals to Treasury, (e) the operational mechanics of glidepath construction under the BlackRock LifePath Paycheck or Goldman Sachs Panorix architectures (treated only at the level of public partnership statements).
