## Foreword

Captive insurance is the oldest tax-and-risk structure being repurposed for the 21st century 1099 workforce. The shape: a self-employed business of one — an EIN, no W-2 employees other than a spouse — joins a regulated captive insurance company, gets covered through a self-funded plan with reference-based-pricing claims administration, and writes off the premium as a business expense. The federal anchor is **IRC §831(b)**, which lets small non-life insurers elect to pay tax only on investment income up to a $2.85M (2026) annual premium ceiling[^1][^2].

Two regulatory shifts in 2025–2026 redefined the perimeter. **IRS T.D. 10029** (90 FR 3534, effective January 14, 2025) designated certain micro-captive 831(b) arrangements as **listed transactions** under Treas. Reg. §1.6011-10(c), with **Form 8886** disclosure required within 90 days[^3][^4][^1]. **Vermont H.649**, signed by Governor Phil Scott on March 24, 2026 and effective July 1, 2026, codified the "gold standard" of the **1,400+ licensed Vermont captives** by prohibiting RRG loans/investments to member-owners and formalizing NAIC-aligned quarterly reporting[^5][^6][^7][^8].

For the 1099 cohort specifically, **North Carolina** is the operational domicile of choice: **263 captive insurance companies + 746 cells/series** at year-end 2025[^9], **21 new captives + ~80 cells/series** approved in 2025[^10], and the **Vault Health Captive** behind **Solo Health Collective** anchored to NC since launch in 2023[^11][^12]. **Florida SB 990 / CS/CS/HB 883** — signed effective July 1, 2026 — opens a fourth domicile with a deliberately modest protected-cell framework[^13][^14][^15]. This paper maps all four layers: federal regulatory perimeter, the multi-state domicile cohort, the Vault Health + Solo + Ten99 vendor stack, and the founder wedges still open as the cohort triples in 2027.

## Executive Summary

Five claims this paper defends:

1. **T.D. 10029 redrew the federal perimeter on January 14, 2025.** The final rule[^3] — 90 FR 3534, RIN 1545-BQ44, TD 10029[^3] — identifies a captive transaction as a[^3] **Micro-captive Listed Transaction** when (i) the insured or related party owns ≥[^3] **20%** of the captive (the "20% Relationship Test")[^16], (ii) the captive financed/conveyed assets to the owner/insured/related party without taxable income over the 5-year Financing Computation Period (the "Financing Factor"), AND (iii) the captive's loss ratio is below[^17] **30%** over the 10-year Loss Ratio Computation Period (the "Loss Ratio Factor")[^3][^4][^16]. A **Transaction of Interest** is identified when 20% ownership + (Financing Factor OR[^17] loss ratio < **60%**)[^17][^18]. **Form 8886** disclosure is required for all open tax years; failure triggers IRC §6707A penalties[^4][^17].

2. **Vermont H.649 reaffirmed Vermont's "gold standard" on March 24, 2026.** The bill, the State of Vermont's annual captive insurance bill, was passed unanimously by the General Assembly on March 12, 2026 and signed by Governor Phil Scott on March 24[^5][^6][^19]. It codifies three changes: (a) prohibits **risk retention groups** from making loans to or investments in member-owners or affiliates (with a January 1, 2026 grandfather clause)[^6][^8]; (b) formalizes **quarterly NAIC financial filings** due May 15, August 15, and November 15[^8]; (c) requires each protected cell to file a sworn certifying statement within **30 days** of commencing business[^7][^8]. Vermont remains the leading captive domicile with **1,400+ licensed captives**[^5][^6][^7].

3. **North Carolina is the operational domicile of choice for the 1099 cohort.** At year-end 2025, NC regulated **263 captive insurance companies + 746 cells/series**, totaling more than **1,000 risk-bearing entities**[^9]. **21 new captives** were licensed in 2025 with approximately **80 cells/series approved**[^10]. The 2025 mix: **188 pure captive insurers, 48 protected cell captive insurers, 9 risk retention groups, and 18 special purpose captive insurers**[^9]. The **2013 North Carolina Captive Insurance Act** provides Insurance Commissioner Mike Causey discretion to regulate each captive according to its risk profile[^9][^20]. Filing perimeter: Form C-200 (Annual Report), Form C-201 (Annual Filings Checklist), Form C-202 (Direct Economic Impact – Captive Insurer), Form C-203 (Direct Economic Impact – Service Provider), Form C-207 (Inactive Captive Annual Update)[^21][^22].

4. **The Vault Health Captive + Solo Health Collective stack is the canonical 1099 captive-health-plan template.** Vault Health Captive, LLC — domiciled and regulated by the North Carolina Department of Insurance — is the captive insurance company behind **Solo Health Collective**, launched 2023 by Healthy Business Group (HBG)[^11][^12]. Plan deductibles: **$2,500, $5,000, $10,000** (also out-of-pocket maximums except tier-2+ Rx co-pays)[^11]. The $2,500 + $5,000 plans are **HSA-eligible**[^23]. **MultiPlan PHCS** is the nationwide PPO network (all 50 states + abroad emergencies)[^11][^23]. **OdysseyRe** (A-rated reinsurer) provides reinsurance with **no specific or aggregate paid claim limit**[^11][^24]. Solo claims **20–50% savings** vs traditional coverage options[^25][^26]. Members must have a federal **EIN**; year-round enrollment with no qualifying life event[^25][^26].

5. **Florida SB 990 / CS/CS/HB 883 opens a fourth captive domicile July 1, 2026.** The bill — passed unanimously in House and Senate, with final approval on March 4, 2026 — creates the **protected-cell captive insurance company (PCC)** framework in Florida[^13][^14][^15][^27][^28]. PCC minimum capital + unimpaired surplus: **$100,000 each** (or $200,000 paid-in for protected-cell captives in some configurations)[^15][^29]. Florida had **3 captives total** at the start of 2026 — up from 1 the prior year — vs Vermont's 1,400+ and NC's 263[^15]. The **Florida Captive Insurance Association** (FCIA) is targeting **200 captives** via a forthcoming statutory reform package and a premium-tax cut from **1.75%** to **~0.3–0.4%** to match Vermont/NC/Tennessee effective rates[^15][^29].

## Part I — The Federal Regulatory Perimeter: T.D. 10029 + IRC §6011 + Form 8886

The federal anchor of micro-captive structures is **IRC §831(b)**, which permits small non-life insurance companies (premiums ≤ $2.85M for 2026) to elect taxation only on investment income[^1][^2][^17]. The structure has long been targeted by IRS scrutiny as susceptible to abuse — captives capitalized purely to reduce taxable income from related operating businesses without true insurance risk transfer.

**Notice 2016-66.** The original IRS classification of certain 831(b) micro-captive transactions as "Transactions of Interest" came via Notice 2016-66[^4][^30]. The notice was vacated by the U.S. District Court for the Eastern District of Tennessee in *CIC Services, LLC v. IRS*, 2022 WL 985619 (E.D. Tenn. March 21, 2022), as modified by 2022 WL 2078036 (E.D. Tenn. June 2, 2022)[^4][^30]. The court relied on *Mann Construction v. United States*, **27 F.4th 1138, 1147 (6th Cir. 2022)**, finding the IRS failed to comply with **Administrative Procedure Act (APA)** notice-and-comment procedures[^4][^16].

**The Proposed Regulations (REG-109309-22).** Issued April 11, 2023 (88 FR 21547), the proposed regulations re-issued the listed-transaction designation through APA-compliant rulemaking[^3][^16]. They obsoleted Notice 2016-66 and considered all comments received in response to the original Notice[^3][^31].

**The Final Rule: T.D. 10029 (January 10, 2025).** Published in the Federal Register on **January 14, 2025** at **90 FR 3534** (RIN 1545-BQ44), TD 10029 finalized the listed-transaction and transaction-of-interest designations effective on the publication date[^3][^4][^16][^17]. The Final Rule narrowed the proposed regulations on three dimensions:

- **Listed transactions** now require **both** the Financing Factor AND the Loss Ratio Factor (vs. either in the proposed regulations)[^3][^16][^17].
- The Loss Ratio Factor for listed transactions was **lowered from <65% to <30%**[^4][^16][^17].
- The Loss Ratio Factor for transactions of interest was lowered from <65% to **<60%**, and the Loss Ratio Computation Period was extended from **9 to 10 years**[^17][^18].
- **Consumer Coverage Arrangements** — transactions between a seller and an unrelated customer purchasing products + insurance — are **exempt** from listed/interest classification (provided the seller-related insurer is not owned by the customer)[^32].

**The 20% Relationship Test.** The captive must be one in which the insured (or a related person) directly or indirectly holds **at least 20% of the voting power, stock value, or equity** in the captive[^16][^17][^32]. This is the gating definition.

**The Financing Factor.** During the Captive's prior 5-year Financing Computation Period (or all years if existence < 5 years), the Captive directly or indirectly **financed or conveyed** assets — including by guaranty, loan, or other transfer of capital — to the owner, insured, or related party (a "Recipient") without resulting in taxable income or gain to the Recipient[^16][^32].

**The Loss Ratio Factor.** Liabilities for insured losses + claim administration expenses[^16] < **30%** (listed) or[^17] **<60%** (transaction of interest) of premiums earned (less policyholder dividends paid) over the **10-year Loss Ratio Computation Period**[^16][^17].

**Disclosure: Form 8886 + Form 8918.** Participants in either type of transaction file **Form 8886 (Reportable Transaction Disclosure Statement)**; material advisors file **Form 8918 (Material Advisor Disclosure Statement)**[^4][^17][^33]. Disclosure applies to **all open tax years** in which the taxpayer participated in the listed transaction or transaction of interest[^33][^34]. Affected taxpayers had **90 days from January 14, 2025** to comply with first-cycle disclosure obligations[^33][^34].

**The penalty stack.** Failure to disclose triggers **IRC §6707A penalties** ($10,000–$200,000 depending on listed vs interest classification + taxpayer type)[^17]. **IRC §6700 promoter penalties** apply to material advisors[^4]. **IRC §6662 30% accuracy-related penalty** can apply to listed-transaction underpayments. **Circular 230 §10.35 covered-opinion rules** govern advisor opinions on listed transactions.

The aggregate effect: micro-captive structures now operate under a continuous-disclosure perimeter where the IRS's posture is "mandatory transparency, not prohibition" — but the disclosure itself functions as a high-friction filter on abusive arrangements.

## Part II — The Domicile Map: Vermont, NC, Florida, Hawaii, Delaware

### Vermont — The "Gold Standard" (1,400+ Captives, H.649 Enacted March 24, 2026)

Vermont's captive insurance industry is the largest by total premium in the United States, with **more than 1,400 licensed captives** and decades of regulatory leadership[^5][^6][^7][^8][^19]. Governor Phil Scott signed **House Bill 649** on March 24, 2026 (effective July 1, 2026); the bill passed the General Assembly unanimously on March 12, 2026[^7][^8][^19][^35].

H.649's three substantive changes:

- **Prohibition on RRG loans/investments to member-owners + affiliates** (8 V.S.A. § new section), codifying long-standing practice; grandfather clause exempts arrangements in place before January 1, 2026[^6][^7][^8].
- **Quarterly NAIC financial statement filings** for risk retention groups, with electronic submission due May 15, August 15, and November 15 each year[^7][^8].
- **Sworn certifying statement** required from each protected cell within **30 days** of commencing business, certifying funding + collateral per the approved plan of operation (8 V.S.A. § 6034i)[^8][^36].

Christine Brown, Deputy Commissioner of Captive Insurance at the Vermont DFR: "This bill demonstrates both the strength of Vermont's laws and our commitment to continually enhancing them"[^8]. Brittany Nevins, Captive Insurance Economic Development Director: "Vermont's approach to captive regulation is rooted in expertise and responsiveness"[^8].

### North Carolina — The 1099-Cohort Operational Domicile

The **2013 North Carolina Captive Insurance Act** (Chapter 58, Article 10, Part 9 of the North Carolina General Statutes) — passed unanimously by the General Assembly — provides Commissioner Mike Causey with discretion to regulate each captive insurer per its unique risk profile[^9][^10][^20]. NC's growth is the steepest in the cohort: **52 captives 9 years ago → 293 at year-end 2024 → 263 captive companies + 746 cells/series at year-end 2025** (over **1,000 risk-bearing entities**)[^9][^10].

**2025 mix**[^9]:
- 188 pure captive insurers
- 48 protected cell captive insurers
- 9 risk retention groups
- 18 special purpose captive insurers

**Filing perimeter**: Form C-200 (Annual Report Form, with Excel CARF), Form C-201 (Captive Insurer Annual Filings Checklist), Form C-202 (Direct Economic Impact – Captive Insurer), Form C-203 (Direct Economic Impact – Service Provider), Form C-207 (Inactive Captive Annual Update Form), and Premium Tax Returns[^21][^22][^37]. Annual reports due **March 1** (or March 15 for pure/association/sponsored/industrial-insured/agency captives) per N.C. G.S. **58-10-405**[^37]. Officer + director changes must be reported within **30 days** per N.C. G.S. **58-10-385(a)** with Form C-101 biographical affidavit[^37].

The 2025 newly licensed captives represent industries including financial services, insurance, transportation, healthcare, construction, and manufacturing — and the relocation of captive insurers from other domiciles to NC[^10].

### Hawaii — The 1986 Tiered-Premium-Tax Domicile

Hawaii's captive program operates under **Hawaii Revised Statutes Chapter 431, Article 19** (since 1986)[^38]. Premium-tax structure: **0.25% on first $25M / 0.15% on next $25M–$50M / 0.05% on $50M+**, capped at $200,000 with a $1,000 application fee. The structure favors larger captives at the high end while remaining competitive at the small end.

### Delaware — The OPTins-Enabled Mid-Tier Domicile

Delaware's Captive Bureau charges a **$300 application fee + $3,200 processing fee** for new captive licensure. The NAIC **OPTins payment portal** became effective for Delaware on **March 16, 2026** for premium tax submissions, modernizing the payment infrastructure for both domiciled and Delaware-license-holding captives.

### Florida — SB 990 / CS/CS/HB 883 (Effective July 1, 2026)

**SB 990 (Senator Leek) / CS/CS/HB 883 (Rep. Fabricio)** received unanimous support in the Florida House and Senate, with final approval on March 4, 2026, effective July 1, 2026[^13][^14][^15][^27][^28]. The bill establishes the **protected-cell captive insurance company (PCC)** structure in Florida — a single legal insurance entity that allows legally segregated companies to receive captive benefits without standalone setup costs[^14][^28].

**PCC requirements**[^15][^29]:
- Minimum capital + net assets: **$100,000** each + **unimpaired surplus** of $100,000.
- Or in some configurations: **$200,000 paid-in capital** for protected-cell captive insurance companies and **$250,000 unrestricted net assets** if incorporated as a nonprofit[^29].
- Must be incorporated as stock insurer / mutual corporation / nonprofit corporation / limited liability company.
- Must file an OIR (Office of Insurance Regulation) application detailing per-cell expense accounting, OIR jurisdiction acknowledgment, and fair-allocation evidence.
- Each cell's assets cannot pay liabilities of any other cell except by approved agreement.

**The Florida Captive Insurance Association (FCIA)**, formed in late 2025–early 2026 with **Ben Stearns** (Carlton Fields of counsel) as secretary, is targeting **200 captives** via a forthcoming statutory reform package[^15]. The current Florida premium tax is **1.75%** vs Vermont/NC/Tennessee's typical effective rate of **~0.4%**[^15]. **Self-procurement taxes** in Florida — which create tax exposure for non-Florida-domiciled captives insuring Florida-based risks — are an emerging incentive for redomestication[^15]. Florida had only **1 captive for many years** until **+2 in 2025** (one redomesticated from Utah), bringing total to **3** at the start of 2026[^15].

## Part III — The Vault Health Captive + Solo Health Collective Vendor Stack

### Solo Health Collective (Healthy Business Group, 2023)

Solo Health Collective is a **self-funded major medical plan** administered by **Vault Admin Services** and backed by a **Medical Reimbursement Policy** issued through **Vault Health Captive, LLC – Series C**[^11][^23]. The plan was designed for entrepreneurs and self-employed owners operating as a "business of one"[^23][^25]. **Healthy Business Group, LLC (HBG)** — NPN 16185783 — is the insurance services provider behind Solo[^39].

**Captive structure**[^11]:
- **Vault Health Captive** = the captive insurance company, domiciled and regulated by the North Carolina Department of Insurance.
- **Vault Health Holdings** = the captive manager.
- **Vault Admin Services (VAS)** = the third-party plan administrator.
- **Solo Health Collective** = the Healthy Business Group brand layer for the freelancer-targeted plan.
- **OdysseyRe** = A-rated reinsurer providing reinsurance with no specific or aggregate paid claim limit (i.e., no coverage cap)[^11][^24].
- **Fairos** = healthcare cost containment partner managing reference-based pricing[^25][^40].

**Plan design**[^11][^23]:
- **MultiPlan PHCS** nationwide PPO network (all 50 states + emergencies abroad).
- Three deductible options: **$2,500 / $5,000 / $10,000 individual** ($5,000 / $10,000 / $20,000 family).
- Deductibles also serve as **out-of-pocket maximums** (with the exception of tier-2+ Rx co-pays).
- **HSA-eligible**: $2,500 + $5,000 plans (V2500 and V5000)[^11][^24].
- **Lively** is the partner HSA provider via Freelancers Union[^24].
- **Clever Health** for telemedicine and teletherapy.
- **No annual or lifetime limits** on benefits[^24].
- **Year-round enrollment** — no qualifying life event required, no open enrollment window[^41].

**Eligibility**[^11][^25]:
- Self-employed individual operating as "business of one."
- Federal **EIN** required.
- LLC, S-Corp, C-Corp, partnership, or sole proprietorship structures all eligible.
- "Generally healthy" (per brief health questionnaire) — Solo selects for healthy populations to maintain captive cost discipline[^24].

**Cost-savings claim**: Solo enables businesses of one to save **20–50%** off traditional coverage options[^25][^26]. The captive structure means premiums become **business expenses** deductible to the captive owner — distinct from the marketplace ACA model where subsidies may interact with the self-employed health insurance deduction[^26][^42].

**Partnership signal**: The **Freelancers Union** featured Solo Health Collective in its October 29, 2024 member education content[^24], representing the canonical partnership-channel model for captive-health distribution to the 1099 cohort.

### Ten99 Health (BCBS National PPO for Locum Tenens 1099 Cohort)

Ten99 Health is a parallel offering targeting the locum tenens (independent physician contractor) cohort and broader 1099 specialists[^43]. The plan structure: **BCBS National PPO** network, **$1,000 / $3,000 deductibles**, monthly premium tiers from **$746 to $2,183** depending on coverage level. The Ten99 stack uses **Section 125 cafeteria plan** structures to enable pre-tax premium deductions where eligibility allows[^44].

### Angle Health — The SMB-Tier Comparable

Angle Health, the digitally-native employer health insurance carrier, closed a **$134M Series B** in December 2025[^45]. While not a 1099-direct provider, Angle Health represents the level-funded SMB benchmark — the comparable structure when a 1099 worker grows into a multi-employee small business and exits the captive-of-one pattern.

## Part IV — Adversarial Layer: 2,200% Growth, IRS Scrutiny, and the "Reducing Taxes Is Not a Legitimate Reason" Critique

The 1099-captive-insurance market sits in a tense zone between substantive risk-transfer innovation and regulatory-arbitrage abuse[^46]. Per Insurance Journal coverage[^46] cited by the SRA 831(b) Admin, micro-captive market growth was[^46] **2,200% from 2019 to 2024**[^46] — driven by the combination of low federal tax rates, state-level captive-domicile competition, and the entrepreneurial gig-economy expansion[^46].

The adversarial signals:

**Unity Captive Solutions critique**: "Reducing taxes is not a legitimate reason" to form a captive — the IRS posture in defending T.D. 10029 is that the captive must demonstrate actual risk transfer + actuarially-justified premiums, not merely tax-deferral[^17]. The Loss Ratio Factor[^17] (<30% for listed transactions)[^17] is the IRS's quantitative proxy for whether the captive is operating as insurance vs. as a tax shelter[^17].

**The 2,200% growth statistic** itself is a flag[^46]. The Treasury Department + IRS preamble to T.D. 10029 specifically rejected commenters' arguments that the proposed regulations existed "to harass otherwise valid businesses," noting that the regulations are intended to require **disclosure** rather than prohibition, and that the factors are "objective and reasonably determined, based on relevant factors in existing statutory provisions, on available industry data, and on a careful review of case law and examination information"[^4].

**Court history matters**. Beyond *CIC Services* and *Mann Construction*, the cumulative judicial record on micro-captives includes Tax Court decisions in *Avrahami v. Commissioner* (149 T.C. 144), *Reserve Mechanical Corp. v. Commissioner* (T.C. Memo 2018-86, aff'd 34 F.4th 881 (10th Cir. 2022)), and *Caylor Land & Development, Inc. v. Commissioner* (T.C. Memo 2021-30) — all of which sustained IRS challenges to specific captive arrangements as failing the insurance-substance-over-form test.

**The "abusive arrangement" pattern** that triggered the listed-transaction designation includes (a) circular financing where the captive lends premiums back to the operating business, (b) loss ratios that imply premiums were not actuarially justified, and (c) related-party ownership structures that preserve the tax benefit of §831(b) while avoiding genuine risk transfer.

For the 1099-captive cohort, the practical implication: Vault Health Captive's **OdysseyRe-reinsured** structure with **no claim cap**[^11], combined with the Multiplan PHCS network's open out-of-network coverage[^23], is designed to demonstrate actual risk transfer — premiums must cover real medical claims that the captive cannot front-load through related-party financing.

## Part V — The Three-Model Framework: Captive vs Platform-Funded vs Cooperative

The 1099 health-benefit market in 2027 splits into three structural models:

### Model 1: Captive Insurance (Vault Health Captive / Solo Health Collective Template)

- **Premiums** are paid by the 1099 business owner to a NC-domiciled captive insurance company.
- **Plan administration** is by a third-party administrator (Vault Admin Services).
- **Reinsurance** is via OdysseyRe or comparable A-rated reinsurer.
- **Tax treatment**: Premiums are **business expenses** deductible to the captive owner; if structured as Mega Backdoor Roth Solo 401(k)-adjacent, the after-tax dollars work doubly[^42].
- **Compliance**: Subject to NC Department of Insurance regulation per Chapter 58 Article 10, Form 8886 disclosure if T.D. 10029 thresholds are met[^21][^33].
- **Cost-savings claim**: 20–50% vs ACA[^25].

### Model 2: Platform-Funded Portable Benefits (Stride Health + Lyft / DoorDash / Shipt Template)

The platform pre-funds a benefits stipend via the rideshare/delivery contract; the worker draws on it through Stride Health partner-coverage APIs[^47]. Per the parent paper, this structure passes through gig-platform-pooled risk without requiring the worker to carry the captive ownership stake.

### Model 3: Cooperative 1099→W-2 Conversion (Opolis / Employment Commons Template)

The Opolis Employment Commons converts the 1099 worker into a W-2 employee of a member-cooperative — making the worker eligible for traditional employer-sponsored group health insurance via the cooperative's plan design. This is the path-of-least-regulatory-resistance for high-billing freelancers willing to absorb the W-2 administrative friction.

The structural trade-offs: **Captive** (deepest tax-advantage; highest regulatory friction post-T.D. 10029), **Platform-funded** (lowest worker friction; thinnest coverage), **Cooperative** (most-traditional benefit design; loss of 1099 flexibility).

## Part VI — Founder Wedges

Six wedges remain open in 2026–2027, ordered by defensibility:

### 1. NC + Vermont + Florida Three-Domicile Captive-As-A-Service Layer

The captive-formation regulatory friction (Form C-201 NC + H.649 Vermont + SB 990 Florida + Hawaii tiered-premium-tax) is the operational moat. A SaaS that abstracts the three-domicile filing perimeter into a single product flow — pick your preferred domicile, generate the application package, file via NAIC OPTins where applicable, manage annual reports — captures the captive-formation lifecycle for the 1099 cohort. Vault's NC-only model is the proof of demand; the unfilled need is a multi-domicile operator with Vermont depth + Florida new-market leverage.

### 2. T.D. 10029 Form 8886 Compliance SaaS

Form 8886 disclosure for listed transactions and transactions of interest is a recurring annual obligation under T.D. 10029[^4][^33]. A specialty compliance product priced at **$299–$999/year** that ingests captive financials, computes the[^17] 20% Relationship Test + Financing Factor + Loss Ratio Factor[^17], generates the Form 8886 PDF + IRS Office of Tax Shelter Analysis (OTSA) submission[^33], and maintains a 6-year retention archive captures the compliance long tail. Deepest defensibility: integration into Vault's + Spark's + comparable captive-manager admin platforms.

### 3. Pediatric / Specialty / Geriatric 1099 Captive Health Plan Verticals

Solo Health Collective targets generally-healthy freelancers via a health questionnaire[^24]. Adjacent verticals — pediatric coverage for freelancer families, specialty-care 1099 (e.g., physician locum tenens via Ten99 Health[^43]), geriatric-1099 (pre-Medicare freelancers age 50–64) — each have distinct risk pools and can support captive-of-one structures with risk-pool-specific reinsurance. The wedge is in pricing the right reinsurance tower for each cohort + curating providers within MultiPlan PHCS.

### 4. Vault-Style Captive Manager for the Long Tail of Solo Cohorts

Healthy Business Group + Vault Health Holdings is the canonical captive manager + plan administrator for the Solo cohort[^11]. The wedge: a parallel manager-administrator stack for adjacent 1099 cohorts (locum tenens physicians, IT consultants, fractional CFOs, gig-economy creators) — each with a domiciled captive in NC, FL, or VT, served by a common administration platform. The captive manager's economics scale with assets-under-administration; the plan administrator's economics scale with claims volume.

### 5. Cross-Captive Reinsurance Pool for the 1099 Cohort

OdysseyRe + Lloyd's-syndicate-class reinsurers price per-captive A-rated covers individually. A reinsurance broker-platform that pools 1,000+ 1099-cohort captives into a single negotiated reinsurance tower — with tiered loss ratios + risk-stratification by cohort — captures the spread between individual-captive and pooled-captive reinsurance pricing. This is structurally the same wedge as PEP 401(k) plans for retirement, applied to the captive insurance layer.

### 6. The Multistate Self-Procurement-Tax Compliance Layer

Florida's self-procurement tax exposure for non-Florida-domiciled captives insuring Florida-based risks is the model[^15]. As more states (CA, NY, TX) consider similar structures, multistate captive owners face an increasing tax-allocation compliance burden. A compliance SaaS that tracks insured-risk geographic apportionment, computes per-state self-procurement tax obligations, and generates state-specific filings captures the cross-state operational layer.

## Part VII — Open Questions and What This Paper Does Not Cover

Five threads this paper leaves open:

1. **The IRC §831(b) premium ceiling for 2027.** The 2026 limit per[^17] $2.85M[^17]; CPI adjustments will lift the 2027 ceiling but the precise number is not yet announced. A future paper should track the IRS announcement and any legislative proposals.
2. **State-level Medicaid expansion interactions.** As 1099 workers' incomes fluctuate, captive-health-plan eligibility intersects with Medicaid expansion thresholds. The state-by-state interaction grid is underexplored.
3. **The full Avrahami / Reserve Mechanical / Caylor judicial record.** The micro-captive Tax Court history is rich but has not been compiled into a single audit-grade reference for 1099-cohort-specific litigation risk.
4. **The Captive Insurance Council of the District of Columbia and other domicile expansions.** DC's captive program, Tennessee's captive growth (cited as having "around 0.4%" effective premium tax[^15]), and emerging domiciles (Connecticut, Texas) deserve their own survey.
5. **The Florida statutory reform package post-SB 990.** FCIA Secretary Ben Stearns indicated more comprehensive reform "ideally in collaboration with the Florida Office of Insurance Regulation" is forthcoming[^15]. The 2027 FL legislative session is the next datapoint.

## Glossary

- **Captive insurance company** — an insurance company owned and controlled by the business or businesses it insures, designed to retain insurance risk that would otherwise be transferred to a commercial insurer[^9][^14].
- **IRC §831(b)** — Internal Revenue Code section permitting small non-life insurance companies (premiums ≤ $2.85M for 2026) to elect taxation only on investment income[^1][^17].
- **T.D. 10029** — IRS Treasury Decision 10029, 90 FR 3534, RIN 1545-BQ44, finalizing micro-captive listed-transaction regulations effective January 14, 2025[^3][^4].
- **Listed transaction** — under T.D. 10029, a transaction with both the Financing Factor and a loss ratio[^16] < 30% over the 10-year computation period, after meeting the 20% Relationship Test[^16][^17].
- **Transaction of interest** — under T.D. 10029, a transaction with the Financing Factor OR a loss ratio < 60% over the 10-year period, after meeting the 20% Relationship Test[^17][^18].
- **20% Relationship Test** — the gating definition: insured or related party owns ≥ 20% of voting power, stock value, or equity in the captive[^16][^32].
- **Form 8886** — IRS Reportable Transaction Disclosure Statement, required from participants in listed transactions or transactions of interest[^4][^33].
- **Form 8918** — IRS Material Advisor Disclosure Statement for material advisors to reportable transactions[^4].
- **Protected cell captive insurance company (PCC)** — single legal insurance entity that allows legally segregated participants ("cells") to receive captive benefits without standalone setup costs; effective in Florida July 1, 2026 via SB 990 / CS/CS/HB 883[^14][^28].
- **Risk retention group (RRG)** — captive insurance company that allows businesses with similar risks to pool together to insure themselves[^48].
- **Special purpose captive insurer** — captive structured for a specific risk-financing or insurance need outside standard pure / association / industrial-insured categories[^9][^21].
- **MultiPlan PHCS** — third-party PPO network spanning all 50 states, used as the provider network for Solo Health Collective[^11][^23].
- **Reference-based pricing** — claim-payment methodology pricing services as a multiple of Medicare reimbursement; used by Fairos within the Solo Health Collective stack[^25][^40].
- **OdysseyRe** — A-rated global reinsurer providing the catastrophe layer for Vault Health Captive[^11][^24].
- **Self-procurement tax** — state-level tax imposed on captives domiciled outside the state but insuring in-state risks (e.g., Florida)[^15].
- **NAIC OPTins** — National Association of Insurance Commissioners online premium-tax payment portal[^49].
